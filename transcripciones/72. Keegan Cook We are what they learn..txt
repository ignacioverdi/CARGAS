Keegan Cook is the head coach of the University of Minnesota's volleyball team. He brought his expertise over from the University of Washington, where he was the head coach from 2015 to 2022.

Under Keegan's leadership, Washington's women's volleyball team clinched four Pac-12 conference titles and had an impressive final-four appearance in 2020.

Coach Cook has been recognized with multiple accolades, including the Pac-12 Coach of the Year in 2020 and was the AVCA Board President throughout 2022 and 2023.

Why did you decide to commit to being a professional volleyball coach? What pushed you towards that decision?

The most impactful moment was when I was in university. As a university student, I was coaching and I got connected with a coach named Rob Browning, who was with the men's national team. He was a technical director. He got hired at my university, St. Mary's College of California, I was friends with some of the players on the team and had helped out at times in the gym, shagging balls.

I was a math major at the time, an aspiring math major, and Rob had a very high technical background. Data Volley was still relatively new back then. And he met me, handed me Data Volley, and said, "This is Data Volley, we'll see you tomorrow at practice." He just gave me the program, and I started playing around with it.

Something about it connected with me, tapping into the analytical side of who I am.

I was probably in my 3rd year of university, coaching youth club on the side, and working with the university team.

I was struggling with my upper-division math courses, such as linear algebra and abstract geometry. They were no longer tangible but very theoretical, writing proofs. One day, one of my professors, who I was close with, said, "Keegan, I don't really think you're a math major. I think you're an arithmetic major," which was a bit of a joke slash insult, implying this was not what I was meant to do.

I was already too far ahead. I finished my degree early and started taking as many sports management classes as I could because that's where my passion was.

I was coaching all day at university and then all night I would coach youth club teams. As I prepared to graduate, I graduated on a Friday and started work on a Monday at my university because they had a position open up.

Here I was, a 22-year-old coaching 21-year-olds. I couldn't have started any earlier. I originally wanted to be a math teacher coaching youth volleyball, but then I realized I didn't want to be a math teacher or coach high school volleyball.

I did want to teach, and I was going to teach volleyball. This was going to be my medium. And I just started very quickly.

What advice would you give yourself as a starting coach? An advice that is so clear and logical for you now, but that you had to develop over the years.

That's a good question. It's easy to give advice to other coaches, but when you frame it that way, what advice would I give myself?

Probably not to worry so much. I think as a young person, you've got a good degree of anxiety about the future and what's next. I've probably spent an inordinate amount of energy worrying about the future. It made me passionate about the work I was doing. I tried to do a great job, but I probably lost more nights of sleep than I needed to.

Things have worked out okay, professionally and personally, but in those years of your life, from 22 to 30, there's a good degree of anxiety and worrying as opposed to just settling in and doing the work.

Not being so future-oriented with your mindset. I think that's advice I would give to any coach or myself because it did work out alright.

Being more present "where I am, when I am" is advice I give to young people and young coaches all the time.

Thankfully, as you get into your thirties, I think some of that anxiety goes away, and you get a sense of what really matters. But that's what I remember about those early years.

What are common mistakes you still see in coaching volleyball?

There are two mistakes that I think exist on the same spectrum. The first one being, young coaches, young people are very eager to get the next job or do the thing that they're not doing.

They have these delusions of grandeur about, "Oh, I'm the second assistant, but the first assistant gets to do the recruiting," or "I'm the first assistant, but the head coach gets to make the decisions."

And so, they're always looking to what they're not doing and they want to move quickly through the coaching ranks. They're very ambitious, but what happens is they eventually find themselves in a position where they can't do the job they've been hired for. They move too quickly.

And it's amazing, there's been a lot of opportunity, a lot of change in NCAA volleyball. And there are a lot of assistant coaches who have never coached as a head coach even in the youth system. They've never had to manage a roster or do tryouts or make cuts or deal with parents.

I just see those mistakes being made on both ends, right, where people are advancing too quickly or thinking about advancing too quickly, or they're advancing too slowly and they're not pushing themselves outside their comfort zone.

Team management. At the start of your season, do you implement team rules or does that grow organically together with your players?

There's a quote that I usually mess up that sets the framework for what we do, which is, "Simple rules lead to complex and intelligent behavior, complex rules and regulations lead to simple and stupid behavior." And I've always believed in that.

So, as far as rules go, it's pretty simple: work hard, be smart.

"Be smart" is a very simple rule that's very subjective but covers a lot of behaviors.

"Hey, were you being smart in this moment?" It's yes or no. With that simple principle, I have had very few player issues in over a decade. Just, "Hey, be smart."

They have a handbook from the university that's 80 pages long with all the different, "if you do this on this day, this many times, this is the penalty," and they're held accountable to that. But we don't spend every day going through that as far as I'm concerned.

Problems don't age well, is another simple principle, which is, "Hey, if something bad happens, I know about it immediately." Because the sooner I know about it, the sooner I can act on it. But when we don't communicate problems, they build and turn into something that can be really disruptive in your life. That's a principle that we live by.

As far as the structure of an organization, because I have four assistant coaches and athletic trainers and all these different people, what I've never liked is a top-down model similar to the military. Where there's a chain of command where I sit at the top.

Because to me, information moves too slowly, and there needs to be too many approvals and too much checking. It's all so dependent on the person at the top that it's slow and weak because I could have my blind spots or biases where I make the wrong decision.

So for us, we have values that guide our thoughts and our behaviors and conversations. Simple values, one of which being, we're committed to growth. So the principle is growth. The value is growth. So our thoughts, the way my coaches talk to our athletes, if it's leading to growth for them on the court or off it, then we're okay.

If it's not leading to personal growth, then it's not words or thoughts or actions that are appropriate.

Another value, which I kind of already mentioned, is conflict agility. We will be agile with conflict. So staff members and players all know that we need to be conflict agile. That's important to me. When bad things happen, it's okay. Let's get it through the system quickly so we can solve it.

Another one might be we're loyal to those not present. It's such an important thing to teach young people who don't realize how small the volleyball world is, that someone can be your opponent one day and a teammate the next. Or a coach can be on the other side of the sideline and then be your coach on the national team.

Young people often, and unfortunately adults, can be disloyal to people not present, they can talk poorly about those behind their backs. Sometimes it starts off well-meaning and then it ends up, they're cutting them down behind their back, and they don't understand how many doors are closing when they do that. So, we're loyal to those not present.

We're loyal to the relationship that we share. The example I would use is, I love my wife dearly and I'm loyal to our marriage. The marriage is what unites us, and I love her, but I'm loyal to the marriage. So, I won't do certain things that would jeopardize the marriage. So for our players, you love your teammate, but your relationship is as teammates.

You have to be loyal as a teammate to certain behaviors. And so, you give your organization values, and you spend time on the front end, explaining them and giving them real examples. Then you don't have to worry about what they're thinking or how they're talking or what they're doing.

Because they're letting those values direct their behaviors as opposed to everything coming from me.

That is the organizational structure that I've found to be most successful in this university space. Get really good people, give them very clear, understood values, and then go do volleyball, and this will work out.

They would often be very redundant with their messaging, whether it was volleyball-related or whether it was values-related.

For someone who had been there for six or ten years, you would sometimes roll your eyes and think, "Oh my gosh, we're talking about this again." I didn't understand until later that it's important to create continuity by coming back to these simple principles and talking about them often. You can't talk about them once and then never again. You have to be redundant in some sense.

The other thing I think about is showing is better than telling. The way an athlete conducts themselves is more important than sitting down with a first-year player and saying, "This is how we do things. Do this, don't do that." Just watch. There'll be times where I'll tell an athlete, "Go do what she's doing. Just do what she does." You'll be fine.

Thinking about the most influential people in our lives, it usually wasn't something they told us but how they lived their lives day to day, their words, their actions. That's the most powerful culture there is, how people treat each other on a day-to-day basis.

I can't remember the most important speech my father gave me in my teenage years, but I do know how hard he worked and how he treated his employees at the restaurant.

He didn't need to give me a speech for me to learn those lessons.

So, what I focus on more is whether the players who've been in the organization a bit longer are behaving and speaking the way they're supposed to. Then, it will take care of itself because it's being modeled for them, but it takes time. We've only been here for one year at Minnesota, and those things are a lot farther along now than they were a year ago, that's important.

When preparing a game, what does your general approach look like and did you ever change your approach? Why did you change and was the outcome beneficial?

At the most granular level, we do three 20-minute film sessions. I think that is where I've settled on being the appropriate amount of time that the players can focus and retain information. So, three 20-minute segments.

One that's mostly based on side out, how our opponents are attempting to side out, one that's based on individual hitter tendencies, and then one that's serve and pass related. That's kind of on the granular level of what we aim for.

In the past, I would do much longer film sessions where we would watch a match by rotation, I would chart every attack on a whiteboard, they would watch me chart every attack on a whiteboard. It was very thorough, but it didn't feel efficient, and it also started to feel like they weren't retaining the information the way they needed to.

So there's a lot more questioning than they're used to instead of just presenting information. There's now a lot more questioning to see if they've retained information, especially in that last film session where you're asking questions that would demonstrate that they have some competence.

But I think one thing I value more is consistency.

Just that the process is the same, that the film sessions follow the same process, that the serve and pass the morning of the match is the same every time. I didn't fully understand it, I don't think, and I still don't, because I wasn't an elite athlete. But there's so much going on. There's so much anxiety and excitement.

And if the athletes can know what's coming, it prepares them to perform more consistently as opposed to doing something totally different for each opponent. I don't think that's great for achieving consistent performance.

So, I really value the same amount of time between meal and match, the same film process, the same practice process, just to give the athletes something that is fixed in their day when there's a lot of other things that are often going on—families coming into town, media obligations, things like that.

Can we make sure that what we control is very consistent, I think, is where I've changed over the course of my career.

And of course, Jim was doing it at an even higher level than I was. I just think I more fully believe in it now than I did when I was an assistant coach. As many of the things are, the longer you're in it..most of the things that we're told, I didn't fully comprehend them until five to ten years into my coaching career.

And now, I have a better appreciation and gratitude for what I was taught by my mentors, for sure.

The future of volleyball, from a technical point of view, how will the game evolve according to your opinion?

The skills that, in the last 20 years, have transformed the game the most, in my opinion. The first was serving. Looking at serving from 20 years ago, it's almost laughable to see what's happening and that teams could receive with two, especially with the ball that couldn't touch the tape.

Just how much serving has changed in the past 20 years is unbelievable. And there's still some space to improve even further with the float serve and the hybrid serve coming from the same toss. We've seen a little bit of that, more in the men's game than the women's game, but just being able to hit those two serves interchangeably without having different mechanics is something very interesting.

The easy answer people would say is the effect of taking away the double on setting and tempo and the aggression of coaches and the willingness to try and run faster offenses. That seems like a likely outcome.

In my opinion, the penalty for going over the center line needs to be much more severe.

Like every sport, in moments where athletes are most vulnerable, the penalty for putting them in danger needs to be substantial. If an attacking player hurts a blocker by going under the center line, there's no penalty that is too severe because people's lives have been changed by that play. And with the speed of offenses and the loosening of setting, the game will end up tighter to the net.

And you have seen in the last five years with the effectiveness of the power tip and the way the game is being played on the men's side, now on the women's side, the benefit of setting tight and the excitement around scoring on a tight set. But the coaches aren't doing the work to teach the athletes how to do it safely.

So there's just, in my opinion, a lot of danger. If we don't make some rule changes around the center line that worry me for the safety of our athletes. And then that's not good.

To have more interesting offenses and more interesting defenses, you'd have to do something about the rotation rules.

That's the only other structure in our game that really limits us in terms of creativity, not being able to put people where we want to put them or allow them to do things that they're great at. We're one of the only sports that does that. Can you imagine if it was like, your running back can only be running back three plays in a row and now they have to switch to being a wide receiver or something like that?

Just the structure of that, to not allow an elite athlete to perform their elite skill over and over and over again. It's interesting, but it's also limiting in terms of what we're doing.

So I don't know if our game needs it, but certainly things feel like we're boxed in, volleyball looks the same a lot of places. I find myself being more interested by watching Thailand and watching some teams that overcome their inequities by doing some different things offensively. Just because you don't see that at the highest levels anymore. So it's fun to see that. I don't know if I answered your question well, but that's kind of where I'm at.

Are you watching any international volleyball? What are astounding player characteristics that you're seeing there?

When you feel overwhelmed or have lost your focus temporarily, what do you do?

The saying I come back to is from Jim McLaughlin, "If you're everywhere, you're nowhere." It took me five to ten years to fully understand it, but it perfectly describes the experience of our current generation of athletes.

They often feel overwhelmed and anxious because they are in many spaces at one time. It's never been easier for them to look at their phone at all the things they don't have and all the places they aren't with all the people they aren't with, and they get overwhelmed very quickly.

So, I've become more passionate about helping them be where they are, when they are. "Be where you are, when you are" is a concept I strongly believe in.
