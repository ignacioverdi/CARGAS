98. Nickie Sanlin: There's no progress without failure.

Nickie Sanlin is McKendree University’s Director of Volleyball, head men’s coach, and Assistant Athletic Director for Impact and Strategic Engagement.

Nickie Sanlin took charge of McKendree’s women’s volleyball team in 2014 and built the men’s squad from the ground up a year later.

Since then, her women’s teams have piled up 135 wins and five NCAA tournament appearances, while her men’s squads have passed the 100-victory mark and are the 2025 MIVA regular season champion.

Coach Sanlin owns two MIVA Coach-of-the-Year awards and this past season became the AVCA’s National Collegiate Men’s Coach of the Year, the first woman to earn the honor. USA Volleyball also chose Nickie to lead the U23 men's team at the 2025 NORCECA Pan Am Cup.

Away from the court, she chairs the AVCA Diversity Development Team and mentors young talent in USA Volleyball’s NTDP pipeline.

In this open and insightful conversation, Nickie shares the experiences and ideas that have shaped her coaching journey.

Key takeaways from this article include:

How can failing help you figure out what you truly love?

Deliver clear and supportive feedback to help players progress quickly and build resilience.

The importance of sticking to solid basics instead of chasing flashy new drills every year.

Thoughts on why today’s volleyball needs flexible skills and bold ideas.

Why owning every loss teaches you exactly how to win next time.

Aiming for the next goal keeps you hungry and stops post-win laziness.

Thinking about future rule changes helps coaches plan far ahead.

We have 50 minutes of video interview (divided over 14 clips) with coach Sanlin in this article. Enjoy!

Enter Nickie...

Nickie, why did you decide to commit to being a professional volleyball coach? What pushed you towards that decision?

“I got fired from a job.”

I went from being a collegiate athlete to... not knowing what I wanted to do next. From fifth grade, around 10 years old, through to 22, I played sports my entire life. And then, once that ended, I thought, "Well, I guess the natural next step is to get a job."

But I was terrible at it.

It just wasn’t what I wanted to do. I was working with people who had never played sports, who didn’t have any sense of accountability or drive. It was just a job for them, a means to an end.

What I’ve learned about myself over time is that I don’t have a gray area. I’m either all in or all out. There’s no in-between.

And at that job? I was bad. I worked at Target corporate. It was not good. Eventually, I got fired.

I had no choice but to move back home. I had two kids under the age of two. And the only thing I knew was volleyball.

So I started coaching. Anything I could. Thirteen-year-olds, boys’ 18s, high school teams, whatever I could get my hands on. Then a collegiate assistant job opened up, and I thought, "You know what? I think I can do this."

At that point, I had no idea about Division II, or college coaching, or recruiting. I just knew volleyball.

I didn’t get the first job I applied for because I didn’t have any experience, and I was so bummed. But then the job at McKendree opened up in 2011, and I got it.

It didn’t pay much. Honestly, it was poverty level coaching. But I loved it. I lived it. Breathed it. Ate it. And that’s when it clicked:

I can wear athletic gear every day. I can do something different every day. I get to compete, even if I’m not the one physically competing.

That was probably the real beginning, though looking back, I had already been coaching for years. In school, I coached all summer. I coached club, camps, I didn’t even go home.

Funny thing, my yoga instructor now was my high school volleyball coach. She brought in a letter I wrote as a senior, 17 years old. She sent me a picture of it, and I read it and thought, “Wow… this sounds exactly like me now.”

Even back then, I was basically coaching my teammates, just with worse grammar, before college cleaned that up.

So I’ve been coaching my whole life. But I had to get fired for it to click.

And that’s something I tell my players all the time: “There’s no progress without failure.”

I got fired. One of you might get fired someday. And it might just be the thing that opens the door to something special.

What advice would you give yourself as a starting coach? An advice that is so clear and logical for you now, but that you had to develop over the years.

It’s not personal. It’s not life or death.

I’m very passionate about what I do, and sometimes, that passion used to completely take over. Back then, I didn’t understand why people didn’t operate the same way I did.

I remember telling my sister when she was heading to college, “You need to be first in sprints. First in drills. Be the best—always. And don’t get distracted—boys are dumb.”

(Obviously, I didn’t take that advice myself at the time…)

But seriously, I was so focused on the intangibles that led to success. I just didn’t realize yet how to balance that passion with the message I was trying to give.

Looking back, I think that intensity attracted a lot of the athletes I coached early on. I was in it with them, running, lifting, playing side-by-side. We weren’t that good, but we were in the trenches together.

I don’t think the passion was a bad thing. But I can admit now that I could’ve tempered how I delivered it. I was very brash, very direct. Still am, to a degree. I’ve always been the type to just say, “Hey, this is what it is.”

Back then, I didn’t get why they couldn’t separate the person from the player. I’d go right for the jugular and think, “They’re athletes, they can take it.” I never paused to consider that maybe, at 18 to 22 years old, they couldn’t.

Now, I’ll preface what I say with, “This isn’t about you as a person, this is about you as an athlete.” And yes, I still push them, but I give them the benefit of the doubt. I understand if it hits them personally, even if they say it doesn’t.

That’s something I’d tell my younger self: not everyone hears things the way you do. Not everyone is wired like you.

I always say, “I played school.” I didn’t love school; I just showed up, went to practice, played in games, worked out, and hung out with friends. That was the life I knew.

At the beginning of my coaching career, I was 100% all-in. Sun-up to sundown—recruiting, emailing, calling, just grinding constantly. I gave every ounce of myself to it. And honestly, that dedication helped get the program to where it is today.

But now, I’ve learned to create more balance. My phone goes off at 9 PM, don’t text, don’t call. (Though, of course, my players still call until I answer. That’s just how it is.) But I take more personal time. I prioritize family.

And I’ve learned that delivering a message with care doesn’t mean backing down. It means explaining more clearly. It means helping athletes understand that criticism of performance isn’t criticism of them as people.

I still hold high standards. I still push hard. But I’ve learned how to temper the words, and that’s made all the difference.

What are the bad recommendations you still hear about coaching or training volleyball?

When I was watching the VNL, one thing that stood out, though I’m not sure this is the “right” answer, is how confused people are about being out of rotation on the toss.

It’s become a real hot-button issue. A lot of people are worried that this is going to creep into NCAA play, and honestly, I hope not. I just don’t think we have the officials to police that consistently or accurately.

But beyond that, there are so many things in our game right now that spark debate, like, how are we teaching ball control? How are we training defense?

We were talking offline about coaches still teaching methods from the ‘80s or ‘90s. And I get it, my coach was a midline passer. I grew up on that. But no one is training midline passing anymore. Everything now is outside the midline.

Still, you’ll hear these little pockets of conversation pop up: Why don’t we just go back to the way we used to teach it? Why doesn’t that work anymore?

And sure, volleyball is one of those sports where there are a dozen different ways to skin a cat. There’s more than one way to train something successfully. But I also think we don’t talk enough about this: If something clearly works for someone, why aren’t we open to doing it that way?

I was at a convention recently, and it hit me how many different ways we are teaching something as fundamental as eye work when blocking.

Is it “ball-setter-ball-hitter”? Or “ball-ball-ball-hitter”?

I sat there thinking, How many variations are we going to create for this?

Yes, there’s nuance in coaching. Yes, players learn differently. But sometimes it feels like we’re overcomplicating things just for the sake of being different.

Right now, though, the toss/rotation issue in the VNL is the one creating the most confusion.

Do you have a particular goal? Maybe a goal that you already reached or a goal that is so hard to reach that it gives you daily motivation?

It’s a two-parter for me. As a competitive person, this season was huge. We won our first conference championship, 12 years in the making.

I still remember my first year. We went 8–22. This year, we flipped it, 20–8. That growth didn’t happen overnight. It came from years of building, grinding, and pushing.

So now the next step is: Can we win the conference tournament? Can we hit those next milestones, one by one? That part is self-fulfilling. It’s the path you expect to climb.

But this year, something happened that I never saw coming: I was named National Coach of the Year.

And to be honest, that was never on my radar. Not even close. It wasn’t a goal I’d written down or thought about. It didn’t feel tangible. I’ve been Conference Coach of the Year before, and that’s great, and it means something. It’s recognition from the nine coaches you play against regularly.

And I’ll say this, it’s a blessing and a curse.

The blessing is obvious: it’s an incredible honor. The curse. It’s the pressure that came with it, especially internal pressure.

I’m the friend people call when they’re considering job changes because I ask wild, grounded questions like: Are you happy? Why are you leaving if you're happy?
Put your ego aside. If you’re doing good work and you love where you are, why change it?

I get why people leave. They want to chase national titles, climb to the highest levels.

But I also know what that world can look like, where success doesn’t guarantee security. Where you could win and still get fired two years later.

I have stability. I make decisions. I don’t have all the bells and whistles, but I love my job. I don’t panic when the phone rings. I don’t flinch when it’s my boss or the university president. My kids can come to work with me. After this interview, I’ll probably go home and finish working from there.

That’s not nothing. That’s rare.

But still, the moment you win something big, the pressure changes.

Now I’m working twice as hard to replicate what happened last season. The personnel are different. The expectations are higher. The pressure, it’s not coming from anyone else, it’s all me.

I feel it even more this summer. I’m head coaching the U23 national team, the first woman to ever do it on the men’s or boys’ side. That’s incredible, but also heavy.

I’m working alongside people like Reid Priddy, an Olympic legend, and Luke Reynolds, who’s had an incredible international coaching career and is now at Pepperdine.

There’s this feeling of: Okay, you broke ground. Can you hold your own now? Can you repeat that success?

And the thing is, while some coaches might let an accolade like this go to their head, I go the opposite direction. My internal voice says, You’re nothing. You haven’t done anything yet.

It’s a hard contrast. But it's real. And even with all that, I still love what I do. That’s the key. I love it.

But now I’m stepping into a new space, because the stakes are higher. It used to be hard work, sure, but it never felt like a chore.

Team culture and team rules. Do you have any methods that you return to every new season? It's a big question, but you can take your answer wherever you want to take it.

I’m a rule follower, but also a ‘ask for forgiveness, not permission’ kind of person.

When I first started, there wasn’t a rulebook. So I went with instinct: If it seems stupid, it's probably stupid, so don’t do it.

Show up on time. Be a good human, basic life skills. Over time, like any coach, I’ve developed a framework. I don’t even like calling them “rules”; they’re guidelines to be successful.

Show up early. Be respectful. Be reliable. College students quickly learn: if you’re late, even by a minute, you don’t practice. That’s a fast and memorable lesson.

But when it comes to culture, it’s something that’s evolved naturally.

I’m not a big believer in forced team bonding. I hated it as a player. Sorry to the coaches who swear by it, but I think it’s just forced fun. I’m naturally introverted.

My volleyball persona is the extroverted version of me. I don’t plan “team nights” or cheesy get-to-know-you activities. I didn’t do it when I coached women, and I don’t do it with the men.

Instead, team chemistry grows from real moments, on the court, on the road, in shared struggles. The long bus rides. The big wins. The brutal losses. That’s where culture lives.

I wear many hats for my players, mom, sister, aunt, stylist, and life coach. And every year, it builds on itself. The players coming in are adopted into something that already exists. It’s us versus the world. It’s volleyball against everyone.

Now, I do meet with newcomers during the first week, a change from my early days. I even have a PowerPoint now: How to Be Successful Here.

It’s cheeky, but real: Don’t do dumb things. And when you inevitably do something dumb, don’t lie about it. Own it. Learn from it. I tell them: the fastest way to get in real trouble with me isn’t making mistakes, it’s being a bad human.

That first week, we get competitive fast. Every Thursday, for the first five weeks, we play what we call Top 14. It’s brutal. We mix lineups and play five or six sets. Then the seventh set, we play with the best 14 players, regardless of age or status, based purely on performance.

That has created a cutthroat culture. Starters get left out. And I’m honest: “You didn’t make it this week. You didn’t perform.”

To them, that moment represents more than a game. It symbolizes whether they’re on the travel roster, whether they’re living up to the standard. And I can’t fully capture on camera just how intense I get about this. I’m passionate. I’m competitive. But I’ve also learned to channel that fire more thoughtfully.

That said, this past season, we played Long Beach, and I was deeply disappointed with how we competed. It wasn’t about the loss. I didn’t expect to win. But I did expect us to fight. To compete.

When we didn’t, I took it personally. I’m the head of the snake. If the team didn’t bring it, that’s on me. I had been trying to meet the players where they were at, give a little more space, adjust to them.

After that weekend, that changed. That Monday, I showed up to practice like a storm.

They call it “Backwards Hat Me.” But the hat goes backwards, and the tone shifts.

I told them: I’m done meeting you where you’re at. You’re now meeting me where I’m at. And I’m up here. If you think you’re working hard, it means you’re not working hard enough.

We talk about being all team, all the time. It’s a hard concept for some to grasp, we are in a hyper-competitive environment. But that’s our culture. And the funny thing is, as they get older, they start to become it. They take on versions of that same fire.

Sometimes they forget, I built this from scratch. I was the only woman coaching men. You know that saying, “Saturdays are for the boys”? I’m the boys. Big Game of Thrones fan over here, so yeah, I’m not the queen. I’m the King in the North.

This whole thing is organic. You can’t teach real team chemistry. You can’t force it with balloon races. You can’t script belonging. They have to figure out who they are, and who they are inside this system.

And I try to mirror real life. In the real world, you don’t hang out with everyone you work with. You don’t all have the same interests.

But you do have to learn how to work toward a shared goal. You do have to respect each other in a shared space. That’s what I teach here. We don’t have to all be best friends. But when we step on the court, it’s all team, all the time.

The future of volleyball, from a technical point of view, how will the game evolve according to your opinion? Into what do you want to see volleyball evolve?

First off, this will probably be short because I’m trying to think outside the box here. Six subs with unlimited re-entry, so not just a single re-entry, could change quite a few things.

I was talking with someone about it: imagine you’ve got those two bic options in a rotation, or you’ve got another outside hitter on the court. Collegiately, that would reshape the landscape, giving more athletes a chance to compete.

Beyond that, unlimited re-entry could make certain positions, maybe even the libero, obsolete. I remember one year at a USA camp, we had an entry rule, and it mixed things up. More chances, different lineups, it changes everything. Middles would probably never have to serve. Not once.

And then there’s the no-hand-pass idea, “No Hand-Pass Summer,” if you will. That could evolve the game a lot.

In college, teams don’t always focus on the technical side of the float serve, and guys get blown up by elite float servers. If hand passes are gone, how does everyone adapt?

When we played Pepperdine in 2022, they had one jump-spin server, but thankfully, we were a float-passing team. You just don’t see that in the NCAA—an entire team hitting flat floats except for one green-light guy who spins.

Those are the two big changes that jump out at me right now.

Best game you've ever seen. What recent or game from the volleyball vaults had a change on your coaching vision?

I have something in mind, but I don’t know what year it was. It was at the beginning of our program. I had just started the program, and, if you didn’t know, I talk about Dan Friend all the time ’cause he is one of my friends.

I consider him a mentor, and they called it “MIVAgeddon” when Loyola and Lewis were in the final, and it was in Chicago.

That, for me, was professionally pivotal, because Dan’s program is everything I aspire to be. He was in a national-championship game, he’s been there forever, he’s built a kind of dynasty.

So here was this little school, Lewis, battling the almighty Loyola, Chicago, and it went a deep five sets, something like 18-16 or 19-17 in the fifth, right down to the wire.

I remember sitting at home on pins and needles watching it. And it’s in Chicago, how else could you script a storyline like that? I wanna say it was maybe 2015, 2016.

But that was the eye-opening moment for me: you can do this. We weren’t very good, but Lewis well, their institution and ours are very similar in size, and I thought, “I can do this. I want to do that. I want to be the center of that. I want our guys to play that way.”

It was inspiring, because, like I’ve said on a podcast before, Dan was my first ever supporter. I don’t know if he remembers it, but at a coaches’ convention, before I even had a team, I told him we’d play club for a couple of years. He said, “No, you need a conference affiliation right away,” and that changed my whole trajectory.

And, like I mentioned, I’m all in, there’s no halfway, and that would not have been great for me.

Later, I went to my administration and asked, “What are you expecting from me?” because I’m not shooting to be fifth in a conference of nine; I’m shooting to be the best. We weren’t good, but that match told me, “If they can do that, then I can do that.”

My bosses didn’t know anything about men’s volleyball, but that was definitely pivotal, not just for Loyola, but more for Lewis. It was exciting, and I can still feel the pit of emotion in my stomach from watching that fifth set, going down to the wire, two teams, a clash of the titans in that era of college volleyball. Absolutely a pivotal moment for me, and for my team.

Matias: Did you actually say—out loud—“What are you expecting of me?”
That question is powerful: if a player or anyone working for you asks, “What are you expecting of me?” you’d better be ready to answer. So, did you phrase it exactly that way, and were they able to respond?

Well, yes at that point we were maybe year three, almost year four in the program, and we were talking budgets. The question was, “Why do you have to go to California every year? Why take these trips?” Back then we weren’t last in the conference; we were hovering around five or six—five to seven—still very early in the process.

So I asked, “What are you expecting of me? What do you want me to do here?"

Because we have to go to California. This is not a vacation. I was trying to balance academics with competition. Our campus had never had a program competing at a Division I level,the California trip wasn’t a spring break trip. We were going to California to play the best, to challenge where we stood.

Yes, it’s fun. But, if they only knew, I don’t do team-bonding excursions. We don’t even sit down in restaurants; we get meals catered.

I was frustrated: “Are you expecting me to be mediocre?” It became an educational moment. They didn’t realize what was possible. Meanwhile, a school three hours away, about the same size, had just played in the national-championship game. I’m thinking, “I can do that.”

Nobody flat-out said yes—“Here’s what we’re going to do”—so I just kept chipping away, pushing the envelope. We started winning a bit more, and they began to see the value. It really showed when we began beating teams and tasting little bits of success.

Like I’ve said, I’m an ask-for-forgiveness-rather-than-permission person. Sometimes that isn’t very tactful, but I pride myself on not mincing words, I say what I mean, and I mean what I say. Maybe that’s not always seen as a “female” trait, but since then I haven’t heard many no’s, just compromises on how we’ll get there.

And the narrative has changed: they once expected us to finish five to seven, and this season we just won the regular season. So now it’s, “Oh, you can do this.”

If you can make the strongest 'starting six' in the world. What would your team look like? Why did you choose these players?

I’ve recently, over the past couple of years, worked with Leandro Vissotto from Brazil and Andrii Dyachkov from Ukraine. The USA is now bringing in former international stars, and I’m intimidated.

I pride myself on feeling I’m on an equal playing field with everyone, but, oh my god, Leandro Vissotto is coming here! He’s one of the best 100 players in the world, fresh off his pro career. And Andrii Dyachkov. And I’m just sitting here absolutely panicking.

Leandro turns out to be the most humble, kind guy. One night a bunch of coaches stayed up with him, until two a.m., and I’m just: “Tell me everything, from age 18 till now. We only have a few hours!” What blows my mind is that he’s asking, “Am I teaching this right?” and I’m thinking, You’re asking me?

Yes, I’m the head coach, but you just finished playing this summer and you’re asking me if you’re teaching the skill correctly?

Andrii’s personality is different, still kind, but very straightforward and direct. At camp he’s teaching the left-sides something. He demos, then spots a kid struggling: “John, where’s John?” The kid raises his hand. Andrii says, “You’re doing it wrong. I told you to do it like this.” I’m standing there, trying not to laugh.

Their backgrounds couldn’t be more opposite. Vissotto, maybe not “naturally” gifted, but gifted early on. Andrii, he went to a volleyball school, wasn’t good enough, and they sent him away. Yet now he’s world-class.

Meanwhile I’m digging deeper into VNL and Olympic matches, ever since watching the Games with my dad.

Then there’s Moni Nikolov in the NCAA right now. He’s playing chess; we’re playing checkers. You might’ve seen that NCAA moment when he said, “I was born for this effin' game.”

It was a highlight, and it’s true, he was born and bred for it. We’ve had great NCAA players come through the international pipeline, but Moni is on another level, maybe because he’s a setter.

That’s a roundabout answer to my “six,” but these experiences have shaped my learning.

I already watched a lot of volleyball; now I’m devouring even more international matches. My players eat, live, breathe the game, watching VNL or league streams on VolleyMetrics.

It didn’t exactly answer the question, but those are some personal experiences that keep pushing me.

We had a talk with Giuseppe Vinci, the founder of VolleyMetrics, on Volleybrains on how VolleyMetrics came about, it' use cases, the future of volleyball and sports tracking and so much more.

If you could have a gigantic billboard anywhere with anything on it, metaphorically speaking, getting a message out to every athlete on the globe, what would it say and why?

“If hard work was easy, everybody would do it.” That’s something we say a lot here.

I’m not small, I don’t shrink. I’m the person I want to be. It’s hard to translate that for athletes, but I keep trying:

If someone tells you, you can’t do it, do it anyway. If someone tells you no, don’t worry about proving them wrong, prove yourself right.

You hear athletes talk about not getting the support they need. You have to find that inner voice that says, you can do hard things. I tell my 17-year-old daughter the same: “You’re built to do hard things.” Honestly, I’d put it on a billboard:

You are meant to do hard things.

Because the culture we live in, especially here, can feel like a quitter’s culture. The moment it gets hard, people quit; they jump into the transfer portal, looking for something easier. But you’re built to do hard things. Those challenges aren’t going anywhere.

Matias: There’s a Polish weightlifter named Jerzy Gregorek who moved to the United States. One of his quotes has become popular lately, you might know it:

“Easy choices, hard life.
Hard choices, easy life.”

I really like that saying.

Another idea to what you said: You can do anything you want, but you can’t do everything you want. In other words, you have to choose, because you can’t do it all.

I’ve said this before. Early in my career I struggled to find the balance between being a mom and being a coach. Then I read this book that honestly pissed me off because it was so blunt: “You can’t be great at everything, so you have to choose.”

That hit me like a light bulb. I always joke that I’m “Mother of the Year, fifth place.” I’m okay with top five. Sometimes I’m on the podium, sometimes I even wear the medal, and that’s fine.

Because I believe if I’m not at least trying to be great in the arena I’m in, I won’t be a great parent either. That’s my choice.

Some parents flip it: they aim to be the best mom or dad first and let the coaching side take a back seat. For me, it was pivotal to realize: You can do anything you want, but you can’t do everything.

I can’t make every school play, can’t always go to the park, because I have to go recruiting trips or to games. I chose, consciously, that to be a better parent, I had to chase greatness here, in coaching.

Athletes and coaches sometimes miss that: you can do the one thing, but you can’t do everything.

A lot of people struggle with that when they start families; some even leave the profession. I always say, “Don’t do it, you can balance it.” But they made their choice.

I could never leave this. I wouldn’t forgive myself. So I’m good with fifth place on the Mother-of-the-Year podium.

What is an unusual habit or an absurd thing that you love?

I love horror, murder stories, true-crime docs, straight-up scary movies, all of it.

Every day in October I watch a brand-new horror film and rate it on my personal Instagram. Five years running, so I’ve got a full archive of scary-movie scores now.

This is how I relax: put on a murder mystery, a true-crime series, something about serial killers. I even read that people who unwind with crime shows are “sociopaths.”

So maybe I’m a sociopath, who knows? But that’s my secret: no rom-coms, no comedies.

It’s basically background noise for me, nothing scares me, because I know it’s just murder on a screen. Even if the movie’s “based on a true story,” it doesn’t get to me. I can literally fall asleep to it. Maybe it’s because I figure there are worse things in the world, so my life doesn’t seem that bad.

I watch this stuff all the time, or I’ll switch to period pieces, Vikings, Game of Thrones, that kind of thing. And, like I said earlier, I’m secretly an introvert. I prefer being alone.

My whole volleyball persona is just that, a persona. Deep down, I’m a loner, and I’m totally comfortable by myself.

How has a failure, or apparent failure, set you up for later success? Do you have a “favorite failure” of yours?

Yes, I do. Two years ago my college team was 1-13 in conference, and we lost 26 sets by three points. We’d reach 22 every set and just hit a wall.

The year before we’d been pretty good, so I thought we’d pick right back up, but we didn’t. We got reverse-swept three times. I can’t even remember what we finished that season.

Every day I made a conscious decision: if we’re going to lose, we’re going to work.

They hated it. Guys came into my office questioning what I was doing. I said, “This is what we’re doing, we’re working hard.” No extra running, all volleyball, skill-based stuff.

I’m a drill-to-completion coach: if I say we’re doing it, we do it until it’s done right, not for time.

We kept repeating, “Pool play doesn’t matter,” because in our conference everybody makes the playoffs. We were 1-13, and we wanted Loyola. They were the one-or-two seed, we were the seven; perfect. Pool play doesn’t matter.

Then everything went wrong. We couldn’t find a bus, ended up crammed on a tiny party bus for the five-and-a-half-hour ride to Loyola. Tight, uncomfortable, but the mantra was, “All these bad things don’t matter; we’re beating Loyola.”

And we did. We won in five. I even wrote the lineup wrong in Set 3, which confused them and somehow worked.

They asked, “Why don’t we run that back?” and I’m like, “We can’t have our opposite in the passing rotation!” But that one win saved the season. We’d lost 12 straight, and that single victory turned everything.

Suddenly the light bulb went on: now they saw why I’d been grinding them every day, on every skill. It carried straight into the next year.

You’d think a team that lost like that would splinter, but 99 percent of the roster returned. The next season we were the best defensive team in the NCAA. All that work while we were losing, that’s why it happened.

Belief in one win is powerful. I definitely kicked their tails every day; they didn’t like me. I’d go home and cry. I had imposter syndrome: Why do we keep losing? But the people in my corner told me, “Stay the course.” and I have learned a lot from that.

So I’d hype myself up on the drive to work: backwards hat, let ’em have it, like it or not, you're gonna like it. They did it every day, and it paid off the next two seasons.

Do you have a book that you like a lot? Which lessons did you get out of it?

Right off the bat, I’ve got four books I lean on, three for coaching and one just for the pure joy of reading, though honestly they all connect.

1. Relentless by Tim Grover
Grover—Michael Jordan’s trainer—sorts people into Cleaners, Closers, and Coolers and insists there’s no such thing as a “clutch gene.” You’re either always on or you’re off, period. That was eye-opening for me because it matched how I already think.

2. Extreme Ownership by Jocko Willink
3. The Dichotomy of Leadership by Jocko Willink
I listened to these most recently. I vibe with the militaristic structure: if something goes wrong, own it. The trick is finding the balance—taking neither too much nor too little responsibility.

4. The Alchemist by Paulo Coelho
This one’s my “just-for-fun” pick. The whole story is about taking risks. I’m not naturally a risk-taker, and the main character’s gambles keep blowing up in his face, yet he keeps the faith. I’m not especially religious, but that message—push forward even when you don’t know the outcome—hits hard. It may not be a coaching manual, but it translates: just go. Head first.

In the last five years, what new belief, behavior, or habit has most improved your life?

I did this on and off in college, and now I’m a heavy journal person. I write the good, the bad, the ugly, everything.

I’ve got three or four years of notebooks stacked over there. I use an iPad now, but it’s not the same as pen-to-paper, so I still keep a real journal.

I jot down how I’m feeling, new drills, random thoughts, anything and everything. Whenever new coaches cross my path, I buy them a journal, too. It’s a place to reflect on the past and set goals for the future, and I find it really cathartic.

Over the last year and a half I’ve also gotten deep into yoga, power vinyasa classes, mostly. I’m not doing Olympic lifts anymore (zero motivation; I’m not training for anything), but I still want a solid level of fitness. Yoga gives me that. I can feel the strength gains and see the changes in my body.

So, journaling is number one, and having an outlet, like yoga, is crucial. For a while I needed something to compete in, but nothing clicked.

I still play volleyball now and then, just not like before. These days it’s yoga, yoga, yoga, and journaling. I’ve bought quite a few notebooks for people and told them,

“Write it down, exactly that thought, that movie quote, stick it in the journal.”

When you feel overwhelmed or unfocused, or have lost your focus temporarily, what do you do?

Learning, first things first.

I keep thinking back to that Long Beach match I mentioned earlier. I knew the problem was me. I’d been thrilled with how we competed against Hawai‘i, then suddenly we didn’t compete. So I asked myself, What did I do wrong?

That’s when I grabbed Extreme Ownership. I didn’t need brand-new drills as much as a mindset shift. I had to stare knowledge right in the face and do something to fix how I felt.

I’m lucky: my support system lets me be sad for a day, then says, “Okay—tomorrow we find a solution.”

My best friend, she coaches D-I women’s volleyball, told me, “You don’t get to crawl into a dark hole.” Everybody hits imposter-syndrome moments, but you can’t stay there.

So I dive into learning. Not just skills or tactics—operations, leadership, ownership.

I run a staff and a team; if I’m unsure, they’ll be unsure. Learning comes first. Journaling, studying, pinpointing where I’m deficient, asking, What got us here? How do I get us out? That’s the cycle.

Thank you, Nickie.
