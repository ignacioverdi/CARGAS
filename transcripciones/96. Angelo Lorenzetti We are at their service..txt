Angelo Lorenzetti’s coaching journey spans over three decades, marked by a relentless pursuit of excellence and a profound impact on the teams he leads.

From his early days nurturing talent in Fano and Italy's youth national teams, to coaching the world's top pro teams and global volleyball stars, Angelo has continuously combined a deep knowledge of the game with a strong commitment to helping players grow.

Angelo's leadership and tactical brilliance has brought success to top Italian clubs, including Modena, Piacenza, Trentino, and currently, Sir Sicoma Monini Perugia.

In May 2025, Lorenzetti achieved a career milestone by guiding Perugia to their first-ever CEV Champions League title, a testament to his strategic acumen and ability to inspire peak performance under pressure.

Matias:
Last time we spoke, I asked you what advice you would give your younger self at the beginning of your coaching career.

And your answer was beautiful. You said, "Dear Angelo, remember that the players are the stars of the show. You’re there to serve them. Your job is to help them bring out the best in themselves." You went straight to the core of it.

Could you share a moment in your career when you forgot about the essence of that answer—maybe because of the stress of a match or the pressure to perform? Or maybe a better question is: what do you do when you realize you've lost sight of that?

Angelo:
Yeah, that’s a good question. I won’t claim to be perfect in living up to that principle, but honestly, I can’t recall a specific moment when I completely betrayed it. It’s something I try to stay mindful of every single day—especially when tension rises or there’s potential conflict with a player.

I really make an effort to practice it daily. Maybe I’ve slipped once or twice, but what truly matters to me is that knowing the principle already means you’re actually living it. That’s the key.

Sure, I’ve had better results with some players and not-so-great results with others—but the question that always stays in front of me is: How do I make sure that this player is the lead actor in the story?

That mindset has become a kind of compass for me. Maybe there’s been a day where I lost track of it momentarily, but I can honestly say I’ve never abandoned it consistently. It’s a strong value—it’s the central principle of my work.

Matias:
So it’s something you really live with every day—it’s always present. Maybe I asked the question just to hear you say that again. 🙂 But it’s important. It sounds simple, right? It sounds like it should be easy to do. But if you don’t keep it front of mind, it slips away—and then you lose track of why you’re doing what you’re doing.

Matias:
Let’s talk about non-negotiable principles in team culture. In a volleyball team with just 12 athletes, when even one player changes, the team changes from the inside, right? So are there things—maybe not just “rules,” but certain conversations you have with players at the start of the season? Do you have fixed rules in place, or are you more the type who says, “Let’s see how things evolve”?

Angelo:
That’s a beautiful question. For me, it’s not just about how I am in the gym—it’s how I interpret civic life in general.

I’ll give you an example: when prisons are overcrowded, and statistics show that people often go back to jail after being released, it tells you something.

It tells you that rules by themselves don’t work.

Before we even talk about rules, I believe we have to talk about principles.

The principles are always the same, but the way we apply them can change depending on the group. For me, the core ones are:

Awareness—It starts with asking, “Why am I here?” It’s like being at school and the teacher calls your name—you're declaring your presence, your purpose. "I’m here to learn."

Responsibility—This is your ability to respond to questions that arise from being aware.

Respect—And not just in terms of behavior, but really valuing what you did the day before. You respect today, and through today, you give value to what you built yesterday—whether it’s in training, in relationships, or anything else.

Commitment—Which is self-explanatory but central.

These are civic principles, not just sports values. They belong in the locker room, on the court, and outside in everyday life. Rules, then, are just tools we use to express and uphold those principles.

In my teams, for example, it’s never explicitly written that you can't be late. But that’s obvious—because in regular life, if I have a meeting with someone, I don’t show up late. So it's not a sports rule—it’s basic respect.

That’s important, for professional athletes especially. Sometimes they think, “I live my life outside, and when I step into the gym, I do my job and that’s it.” But it doesn’t work like that—it’s all connected.

If you don’t live those principles in the gym, I think you'll struggle to live them outside too.

Now, that doesn't mean everyone always follows those civic “rules.” But if you work on the principles, you know those principles can be shaped and adapted. I’ve been coaching for decades—I’m 60 now—and I keep meeting new generations.

Take phones, for instance. In volleyball teams, phones have always been a mess. You’re not supposed to have them at the table, not here, not there. But today? Phones are part of our lives. Some coaches still ban them completely. I prefer to go with common sense. When you’re at dinner with your wife and kids, you don’t scroll through your phone unless it’s something urgent. That’s the idea I try to bring into our team too.

Last 2 points of the Champions League win!

So no, it’s not that rules aren’t important. Every structure needs rules. But the problem is that the usual approach to rules is punishment.

We see it in society too—especially in Italy now. Something happens, and the immediate reaction is: new rule, punishment, punishment, punishment.

Instead, I believe things should be explained first. Whether it’s citizens or players, they need to understand why a rule exists.

A rule should help you fulfill a principle. That’s where its value comes from.

Yes, it’s a longer process. It would be easier to just hand out a sheet of rules at the start of the season. But that’s not how I work.

Matias:
I think you’ve already answered my next four questions. I love it. Let me take a look now…

Matias:
So, how do you build a culture where players really take ownership of the team’s success? We’ve touched on this already, right? But it feels like it’s part of something bigger.

Angelo:
Yes, it’s tied to the whole discussion around expectations, you know?

This is actually one of the first conversations I always have with the team—about roles.

Because, let’s be honest, the world of volleyball isn’t always that professional. Many times, as a coach, you walk into a team where you're not necessarily the one who selected the players. Maybe you haven’t even spoken to them before. Sometimes it’s someone from the club, a director, who’s had the conversation with them—and maybe they painted a bit of an ideal picture.

So yeah, on this point I’m very clear. I’m determined. Because I believe that every structure—not just a sports team—rests on two key things:
Respect for roles, and productive conflict.

Now, when we talk about roles, we have to understand what that really means. It's not just a label. It’s a broad concept, but inside that concept are expectations—the player’s expectations of himself, what his teammates expect of him, what I as the coach expect, and what he expects from his coach and teammates in return.

And these expectations? They change every time someone enters a new team environment. That’s why it’s so important to make them explicit.

Because if you don’t, each person starts to construct their own version of reality. They show up thinking things are one way, but reality might be another. And that’s where a gap forms—a misalignment. And to fill that gap, what usually happens? People start to complain. It’s natural.

So one of the first things I make clear is: Who is a starter, and who isn’t?

What does it mean to be a starter? What does it mean to be a backup? Are you a backup who’s close to the starters or one who’s far from them?

That’s something I always clarify right away. If there’s uncertainty, I'll make it clear. But I don’t say something like, “You’re all starters.” If I’ve got three outside hitters and I’m still figuring things out, I’ll say: “None of you is a starter right now.” Because being a starter is something you have to earn.

And in a position like outside hitter, you can’t have three starters. If I’m rotating between players, it means I’m still looking to see if someone can take that role—and that’s something I need to be very clear about.

Later in the season, maybe I’ll tell someone: “You’re the starter now.” But if things change, I have to be transparent. I have to declare it again.

That’s the first thing I do—because clarity is everything.

Also because, when I declare my expectations—and when those expectations are compared with the ones the player and the team hold—then, if problems come up later, I can go back and say: “What did we say at the beginning? What’s changed?”

That gives me a place to start. Because otherwise, everything is just driven by the result. And I believe that the relationship—between me and the player—shouldn’t depend on the result.

It should depend on our relational skills, on how we manage and nurture that connection. That goes beyond performance.

And this is especially true when working with younger players. It’s not that it’s difficult—it’s actually something really beautiful. But it’s not enough to just say it. You have to practice it consistently.

Another really important concept for me is making sure the players know that I’m available. This matters a lot to me.

I’m always at the office, in the gym. I know you come from a place like Belgium, where maybe that’s more common—but here in Italy, in our sports halls, that wasn’t always the case. Today it's becoming more normal, but when I started, it wasn’t. Coaches often worked from home.

And that’s something I couldn’t stand—because the players need to know where to find me.

It’s not just about logistics—it’s about the idea that the team is a 'place', an environment, not a “non-place.”

Let me explain that a bit, and you can cut what you want later if I go on too long—but I think it’s important.

In everyday life, we spend a lot of time in “non-places”—cinemas, airports, train stations. They look like real places, with walls and structure, but they’re not. Because in those spaces, you step in, take on a temporary identity—passenger, customer, spectator—you do what you need to do, then you leave and pick up your real identity again. Nothing sticks.

But a team, a gym, is a place. It’s where I come in with my full identity—and I exchange that identity with the people I’m working with. And when I walk out again, I don’t leave unchanged. I leave with a slightly different identity. Ideally, a better one. And that takes work.

So it’s important that my players know: I’m here for that work. I’m present. That doesn’t mean I’m always agreeing with them—but I’m here, especially for the process of asking questions.

This kind of availability is something I try to help them understand as a value—something you cultivate, respect, and prepare for.

For example, when I call a player in for a meeting, I don’t just say, “Come to the office, I want to talk.” I say, “Come tomorrow, I’d like to talk about this, this, this, and this.”

And I expect the same in return. When a player comes to me and says, “Coach, can I talk to you for five minutes?”—I always say, “If you don’t tell me what you want to talk about, I’ll listen, but I won’t respond. Because I’m not prepared.”

So for me, dialogue—real dialogue—isn’t just this quick, reactive back-and-forth. It’s not pam pam pam. It’s something I have to prepare for.

Because when I prepare, I ask myself questions. I ask, “Where do I want to take this conversation?” Then I share those questions with the other person, so they can prepare too. So they can ask their own questions.

That process of being inside the question—that’s what helps us eventually find the right answers. But before we get to answers, we have to go through a lot of good questions.

That, for me, is how I live inside a team. That’s how I show up.

Matias:
No, really—this is all very concrete, very real, and also really beautiful. You could even say this is your way of translating what a “non-place” actually is. In a non-place, you’re almost like a product. You’re used, there is simple exchange, and then you move on—and someone else comes in right after you.

But in a place/ an environment, you’re part of it. You have an impact on it.

Angelo:
Exactly. Yes, I always say that sport, maybe more than anything else, is about having a sense of belonging.

If I can play with words a bit, I think of it like inhabiting a space—like living in an apartment building.

Living in an apartment building is nothing like staying in a hotel room. In an apartment building, you live with others. There are responsibilities, rights, conversations. And that’s exactly what a team is.

It’s not something built with walls. It’s a space we inhabit together. We live inside it. And that means even when we’re traveling, staying in hotels, we’re still inhabiting the place that is the team. It’s always with us.

Matias:
Right. We’re kind of playing with words here, but I really like it.

Matias:
Let’s go to the fifth question I had written down. How do you help players recover mentally from mistakes? So now we’re talking a bit more practically, as I mentioned earlier. Do you have a specific strategy or a certain way of talking to help players bounce back quickly?

It kind of overlaps with what you were already talking about, right? So maybe we’re not changing topic completely. Maybe you’re already thinking, “Yeah, this connects to what I said before.”

But let’s go deeper with a specific example—what do you do when a player is in crisis? Does it depend on the individual? Or do you have a set of questions or an approach you usually take?

Angelo:
There are really two important aspects here.

First, I should say that I come from a particular cultural background. I’m sixty years old now, and I was raised in a Catholic culture—a culture of school teachers who separated students into “good” and “bad.” That’s something I’ve had to work on deeply, starting with my own mistakes, in order to be able to support others effectively in the gym.

I can’t say that this process is finished. It’s still ongoing.

But the idea is always to create a safe space for mistakes.

That’s especially important in volleyball. Because even the best performance is never 100% .

Even inside your best game, there are mistakes. So we need to create a space where the mistake can exist safely, where we can work on it.

The hard part is figuring out whether the player actually feels safe in that space.
Because a lot of players experience a mistake as a judgment—not just of their play, but of their identity, their worth as a person. And that makes everything harder.

So it’s essential to learn how to give useful feedback. That takes time and awareness. But also, we have to recognize that not all mistakes are the same.
There are mistakes that are useful—they help us improve. And then there are others that come just from carelessness, from lack of attention.

And we need to distinguish between the two.

Still, the most important thing—though I don’t always manage it—is to first bring the mistake into a safe place. That means: "If I make a mistake today, my life doesn’t change. My value doesn’t change. My relationship with you doesn’t change."

It’s something we’re going to work on. Because we’re in a space of learning. That’s the frame.

The challenge, though, is that volleyball seasons move so fast. There’s not always time to pause and create that space the way we’d like.

We’re also fighting against time, right? We want everything immediately.

That’s why one of the first things is to establish priorities. Like: first, we do this. If there’s time or space, then we do that. But we can’t do everything at once. And that’s something we really have to work on—but not with the so-called "wild" players, the ones who are a bit more rough around the edges.

Not with the hotheads, the ones who might act out a bit. But with the very disciplined ones, the “good kids.”

Because those players—often the most precise and responsible—believe that being demanding with themselves is an extremely high value.

And yes, being demanding can be good, but it also brings a big trap: the pursuit of perfection. And that can become self-defeating.

We should be aiming for excellence, not perfection.

And ironically, excellence is often reached more easily by players who are a bit more… loose, a bit more relaxed.

Take Ngapeth, for example. His strength isn’t that he’s technically perfect. It’s that, for him, making a mistake is like a challenge—a game. He never sees a mistake as a judgment on who he is. And that makes him powerful.

Same with Giannelli. Especially when it comes to decision-making, mistakes just don’t throw him off. For him, a mistake is fuel. It’s a spark. It drives him forward.

Now, with players like Ngapeth or Giannelli, sometimes you actually have to pull them back a bit—remind them, as Julio Velasco once told Fefè De Giorgi: “You have to play volleyball, not play with the volleyball.” That’s the line.

But for the others—the ones who are too hard on themselves—you have to help them loosen up. You have to help them see that saying, “I’m just really demanding with myself,” isn’t a strength. Sometimes, it’s a shield.

And honestly, when I hear that line—“I’m just really demanding with myself”—it makes me nervous. Because that mindset slows down the learning process. Before you can move forward, you have to unblock that inner narrative.

And often, that story doesn’t come from sports. It comes from somewhere else. From your upbringing, from your family.

I know this from personal experience. I grew up in a family where doing well was just expected. It was the baseline. So anytime you made a small mistake, you were penalized. You were made to feel less-than.

And what does that create? When you go out into the world, you’re full of insecurity. After you heard somebody praise you a couple of times—“Good job,” “Well done”—you begin to think, “Wait a second, maybe I do have value. Maybe I can do something.”

But it’s a long and exhausting road.

That journey could be done differently. It doesn’t have to be that hard. And that’s something I try to help players understand.

Matias:
The last 48 hours before a match—It doesn’t have to be an important match. Just a match. I’m guessing I’m not wrong in thinking that even the first match of the year is often approached almost like a playoff match, right? Especially at this stage of the season. (recorded this in March 2025)

Angelo:
Well, for us, it’s almost always “48 hours before” because we usually play two matches a week. So this is a good question—and I have a kind of framework I follow.

The first thing I do is ask: Are there any internal or external voices—things players are hearing—that could be creating limiting beliefs?

That’s especially important in big clubs. For example, there’s one word that I really can’t stand, and it keeps getting thrown around by some people in the organization.

The word is: “We are a strong team.”

Perugia is always labeled as a strong team. But that idea can become a trap. Because being “strong” sounds static. Like something fixed. But what we need to be is good.

It’s like in school: I don’t need to be “intelligent”—because intelligence comes from mom and dad. But being “good” means I’m working at it. I’m studying.

So depending on the environment you’re in, there are all kinds of messages—internal and external—that can lead you into a match with beliefs that make no sense at all.

My first job is to identify those beliefs and throw them out. I tell the players: “Let’s clear the noise. Let’s look at which beliefs actually help us.” That’s the priority.

For example, during playoffs, this kind of talk is common: After Game 1—“Oh, but if we win Game 2, we can shorten the series,” or “If we lose, it gets long…”
But no. The only real goal in a best-of-five series is to win three matches.
That’s it. Doesn’t matter if it’s the first, the second, or the fifth.

This is especially important when you’re coaching a favored team. The mindset needs to be unified.

So the second key thing for me is this: Create a shared interpretation of the event.
"This match, in the context of our journey, has these characteristics. It matters because of this. This is what we need to get out of it."

That’s the starting point.

Then comes the analysis of the opponent.

We begin by looking at their strengths. And we ask: “Are there any strengths they have that we simply can’t do anything about?” If the answer is yes—then those moments will come during the match. And they shouldn’t cause panic or drama when they do. That’s where we need to allow ourselves to be vulnerable.

There are some strengths the opponent has that you can try to limit. And it’s important to be clear about which strengths those are.

Then there are their weaknesses—and if you have the right tools, you need to exploit those.

So those are the three key areas: the strengths you accept, the strengths you can limit, and the weaknesses you can target.

The fourth aspect is more about the competitive management of the match.
Because teams start a series, and maybe they lose the first match, then another—things never play out the same way. There are different rhythms.

So sometimes, during certain phases of the season, you set a goal like:
“It’s important to start strong in the first set,” or “It’s important not to give up three points in a row.”

But this approach is never fixed. It doesn’t depend on the opponent.
It depends on your own journey.

That’s why we always say: regardless of who we’re playing, our path continues.
Because our path—our growth—isn’t dictated by the calendar. We decide our path.

And then there’s one last thing I do, which is a bit more personal.

I take some time to study the posture I need to have in that specific match.
Do I need to be more supportive? A bit more distant? A bit more involved? I try to anticipate that for myself, and I even prepare some questions in advance—for myself.

So yeah, it’s a mix. A balance between mental preparation, tactical awareness, personal reflection, and so on.

Matias:
Right. And I think—correct me if I’m wrong—but I feel like you’ve already answered the next question I had in mind.

Matias:
Let’s talk about high-pressure situations during a match—real game-time tension.
How do you calm your team in those heated moments? Or do you actually like to channel that energy and use it?

I guess it connects with what you were saying earlier, right? It’s about how your team reacts in the moment—but you’ve already prepared for that situation beforehand, so when it comes, you’re not wasting extra energy trying to figure out what to do. You’ve already decided how you’ll respond.

Angelo:
Yes, exactly. I work on that—though I don’t always succeed. Being able to handle that with your team in every phase of the season isn't guaranteed.

But I really like using this metaphor—I even told it to the team again this year—the story of the chalk.

The chalk stick you use to write on a blackboard—it can write important stories, just like a pen. But unlike a pen, it doesn’t leave 'scars'. You can erase it and write again.

A pen? Once you’ve written, sure, you can cross it out—but the mark stays on the paper. It shows that you've erased something.

That metaphor, for me, is powerful. In a match, it means that what happened can’t be denied, but it can be erased—so it doesn’t leave a mark on the next ball, on the next play.

That’s an image that really helps me—the blackboard I can wipe clean. Start fresh.

Matias:
That’s beautiful. You’re saying way too many things I want to steal for my own work life. I really love that.

Matias:
Giving responsibility to your assistant coaches—Is that something you’ve always done, or not? Have you changed over time? Maybe it also depends a lot on them, not just on you. Is it something where you say, “I always want to share things, I want their input and feedback”—or is it more situational?

Angelo:
That’s something that’s changed a lot for me compared to the beginning of my career. At the start, you kind of feel like Zorro—you think you’re the only one who knows the truth. That’s how it is in the beginning.

But over time, I’ve come to realize—and I explain this to my assistants—that delegation is not just about handing out tasks. It’s something deeper. It’s a core value.

If I delegate, it means I have solid foundations. And those foundations need to be built. Because it’s one thing if I’ve been working with an assistant coach for five years, and another if they’re brand new.

The foundation is, first of all, trust. And trust, for me, stands on three legs.

The first—maybe the most important—is competence. And that takes time. It’s also specific competence—what I need. You might bring me your skills, but we have to work on it together. That kind of trust doesn’t happen overnight—it builds through shared work.

Then comes reliability. If you say, “I’ll take care of this,” I need to know that you actually will.

And the third is sincerity. This one is really important in sports like volleyball. We’re not like football, where head coaches bring their entire staff with them. In volleyball, when you join a club, you often walk into a pre-existing staff—people who’ve already seen many coaches come and go.

So you have to ask yourself: are these people still here because they’re truly good, or for other reasons?

Once those three legs—competence, reliability, sincerity—start to stand on their own, then trust begins to rise. And that’s when you can start to delegate meaningfully.

The other important thing is alignment. Not just technically—but in terms of vision.

I want my staff to reflect a shared philosophy. For example, I want them to show the players a spirit of hard work—before anything else. Even more than technical expertise.

Because technical skills depend partly on talent. But the culture of work—that’s something we all have to build and embody together.

Work ethic depends on our own will. Players need to see that we’re working hard—for them. Hopefully we’re working well too, but they need to see that we’re putting in the effort. That should be part of our alignment.

If a staff member shows up at 10:30 in the morning, well… you get what I’m saying. That sends a message.

And then there’s communication. The way they speak—their words—carry weight. There are some assistant coaches who fall into the role of “Father Cristoforo,” like a confessor or priest. And that role can exist in a team, sure. But it has to be assigned. You can’t just claim it for yourself.

Especially in teams with important/ famous players, there’s a natural desire to be close to those guys, to connect with them. And that’s fine—but it needs to be monitored and aligned.

So if there’s trust, and work ethic, and alignment with our values, then yes—I can delegate.

That’s something that wasn’t there at all earlier in my career. Now it’s there more often. And I hope to keep accelerating that learning process with my assistants. I’m getting older, I have less energy, so I need to speed up the process of getting them up to speed.

But the first thing I always say is this: delegation is not just handing out tasks. It’s a process. One that stands on solid foundations.

Matias:
No, I really liked the image you used—the three legs. All three are important. Because if only two are strong and the third is missing, it doesn’t hold. It just doesn’t work.

Angelo:
Yes, because when trust breaks down—and you’ve been a player too—you know what it feels like. When I sense that a player doesn’t feel trust coming from me, I say, “Okay, let’s use the image of the three legs. Which one isn’t holding up?”

Because if one leg isn't solid, we can focus just on that. We don’t need to speak in generalities. We need to be precise.

Do you think I don’t trust you because I don’t see you as competent?
Or because you say you’ll do things, and then you don’t follow through?
Why?

Or maybe it’s not even about volleyball—I don’t trust you because of something else, something off the court. Because you say you’ll do something, and then you don’t.

So if we want to rebuild trust, it’s not about improving your passing or your attacking. It’s about going to work on these other things—those are the real foundation points we need to fix.

That, for me, is something that really helps. It gives direction, and clarity.

Matias:
I want to go back to a question I asked you four years ago—and your answer back then. Let’s see how your opinion stands today. The question was about changing the rules. What’s one rule you’d like to see changed in volleyball?

Last time, you proposed a few ideas. The first was having seven players on court during the break point phase—not sure if you remember that. The second was no net fault calls, so the game wouldn’t stop for minor net touches. Kind of along the same line, you also said fewer interruptions in general, like fewer timeouts. Only one timeout per team and one challenge. And the fourth was a different scoring system: sets to 15, and whoever wins four sets takes the match.

Have your views changed since then? And if you could only choose one of those rules today, which one would it be?

Angelo:
Well, in general, I still look at all of this through the eyes of the viewer at home.

We have to think about volleyball as a show—something engaging. And for it to be truly engaging, it shouldn’t matter whether the person watching supports one team or the other. It needs to be visually appealing.

Waiting for timeouts, video challenges, or long protests—those things make non-fans change the channel. I think there’s a lot of room for improvement there.

As for the seven-player rule, I still believe it’s worth trying. I’ve never liked the idea that middle blockers always rotate out. And it would allow for more defense—more action on the court.

Regarding scoring, I’ve come to a new idea over the years. Who says all sets need to be the same length?

We’ve always talked about reducing match time—sometimes for TV broadcasting, but also because sitting in a venue for three hours can be tough. Still, we have to consider that a match can end 3–0. And when that happens, someone who bought a ticket or a season pass ends up watching something like a mini-series episode that lasts 15 minutes. It’s just not enough.

So the idea I’ve developed is to maybe alternate set lengths. For example: first two sets to 25, third and fourth to 21. Then a fifth set as the decider. That way, you can control match duration a bit better.

If one team is much stronger, the match still offers a meaningful experience for the audience in terms of time and value. And if the match is more balanced, it naturally stretches longer.

It also helps the weaker team. If they’re down 0–2, the next set is shorter—more manageable. That happens often: the underdog stays close to 20 points, then falls off. This way, even if they’re down 2–0, it’s like a new kind of game starts. It changes the rhythm, gives new life.

So yes, I think today I’d choose to change the scoring system. The other ideas? I still believe in them, but that’s the one I’d prioritize now.

Matias:
This was one of the questions I asked you four years ago. The question was: What’s the future of volleyball—how will the game evolve? It’s a big, open-ended question. Last time, you said the game would become faster and more powerful. Players would keep developing, and more complex techniques would be invented.

Now maybe asking about the next decade is too much—but today, what are the skills you think have already grown in importance over the last few years, and which ones will become even more important going forward?

Angelo:
The forearm pass—the bagger—is fundamental.

Because rallies are getting longer and longer, and that means there are more and more 'bagger' touches. And that includes everything—covering, defense, reception, setting... easy sets, and also the tough ones. You can’t reach a high level today without having at least an acceptable bagger.

And yes, I said it before—speed will keep increasing. But now we’re also seeing that teams are finding ways to adapt. So even with all this speed, rallies are lasting longer. And that’s going to keep happening.

What’s really important now—this is something I don’t think should fall only on the people in charge (governing bodies), the ones making decisions from the top—it’s also an ethical responsibility for us coaches.

This faster, more intense volleyball demands players who are better physically prepared. But I feel like we’re going in the opposite direction. We’re pushing to bring in younger and younger players, getting them to play right away.

But just because someone has technical talent doesn’t mean they’re physically ready.

There are too many teams—and even national teams—competing in high-level events without their most talented players, because they’re injured or are in need of urgent rest when it comes to those events.

And for the sport, that’s not good. It’s like putting on a big theater performance—only when the curtain goes up, your best actors aren’t there, and someone else stepped in.

At the highest level, on these global tournaments, we should always have our best athletes on the court. But in the last few major tournaments—both club and national—many of the top teams were missing key players.

That’s a warning sign. And again, I don’t think that’s only on the people at the top—it’s on us coaches.

Sometimes we need to say: stop.

A player needs to be physically prepared before he gets to play the game It’s not just about “playing to learn how to play.” Because yes, maybe you learn by playing—but if that leads to injury, then in the end, you can’t play or compete at all.

That, I think, is the next frontier: Bringing an ethical mindset to our work—not just avoiding doping, but understanding that the future development of a player is part of our professional ethics.

Even if we know a player won’t be in our club team in two years—if in two years time he’ll be one of the best in the world and he should be playing in a major event, then he needs to be there.

And if he’s not—because of something I did—then I’ve taken something away from the sport.

Matias:
Let’s stay on the theme of player development. What’s one training method or approach you used to believe in, but don’t anymore? It can be something very specific—even a technical detail, like a certain type of contact or touch.

Angelo:
It’s not that I no longer believe in it—I’ve just had to adjust. I come from the school of “a thousand repetitions.” And of course, you can’t learn without repetition.

But I’ve realized we need to be constantly looking for faster learning methods.
That has to be part of our ongoing work. Because we’re always fighting against time.

We don’t have much of it—so the idea is: how can we improve and learn as much as possible in less time?

That also ties into physical health. If I can help a player learn with fewer jumps, they get hurt less. They last longer. Their career can be longer.

So when it comes to coaching, yes—we still study technique. But we also need to study neuroscience. To understand how effective our feedback really is. What actually reaches the brain? What sticks?

So it’s an ongoing process—this constant search for more efficient learning, faster learning. That’s where I’ve evolved.
