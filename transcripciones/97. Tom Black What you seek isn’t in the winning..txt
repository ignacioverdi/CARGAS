97. Tom Black: What you seek isn’t in the winning.

Tom Black is the head coach of the University of Georgia’s volleyball team and a former U.S. Women’s National Team assistant who helped win a World Championship in 2014 and an Olympic bronze medal at Rio 2016.

Tom Black played as an outside hitter at UC San Diego from 1993 to 1996, set several school hitting records, and was named Volleyball Magazine’s Small College Player of the Year in 1996.

After college, Tom coached the UC San Diego women's team from 2005 to 2009, guiding them to the NCAA tournament each season and earning Pacific Region Coach of the Year honors in 2006. He then spent seven seasons at Loyola Marymount, making three NCAA trips and reaching the Sweet 16 in 2015. Since 2016, he has led Georgia, steering the Bulldogs to three NCAA berths and consecutive 20-win seasons.

Coach Black has made his mark on the international stage, too. As a U.S. assistant from 2013, he helped the team win its first World Championship in 2014 and an Olympic bronze in 2016.

In 2019, he took charge of Canada’s women, leading them to the Challenger Cup title and a 2020 Nations League spot. Back at Georgia, he earned SEC Coach of the Year honors in 2022.

In this open and insightful conversation, Tom Black shares the experiences and ideas that have shaped his coaching journey.

Key takeaways from this article include:

Reinforce core values in daily micro-moments to let culture take root

Aim scouting at offense and serving where it moves the scoreboard fastest

The one piece of advice he’d give his younger coaching self—and why it probably wouldn’t have worked

How creativity and coaching overlap—and why you’re more creative than you think

Can you enforce team values without making it about yourself?

What’s the biggest time-waster in volleyball prep—and why do so many coaches still do it?

and so much more...

Enter Tom...

Tom, when did it click for you that you wanted to become a full-time, professional volleyball coach? What was that trigger?

Well, a lot. For a long time in my twenties, I was balancing being a coach or being a writer. And honestly, it's not like a huge story. I remember running a drill for a high school team that I was coaching, and things were just clicking in a way that was kind of telling me that I should pursue it.

At the same time, I do think I was chickening out a little bit—like, coaching was safer. And so I think there was part of me feeling like I should go this route because it’s safer and acknowledged.

But I also remember that moment of running the drill and just feeling like I was kind of a part of something, and it was clicking.

So it was just a moment. It was LA Baptist. I was running a defensive drill in the San Fernando Valley. So I still remember that moment, you know? But there were a lot of reasons that I went down that path.

What advice would you give yourself as a starting coach? An advice that is so clear and logic for you now, but that you had to develop over the years.

The hard part with that question is, I know I wouldn't have listened. I think that's the tough part, I just wouldn't have listened. But who I am now, I probably would’ve tried anyway.

And I think there are two parts to that question, too. There’s what I would tell that person based on what I’ve learned, and then there’s also what that individual needs in the moment.

I’ll try to answer without going off on a tangent.

For instance, I think what I would’ve told that person is: there’s no amount of winning or championships that’s going to give you what you think it’s going to give you.

But I also think that, at that time, I would’ve wanted to listen. I would’ve tried. I just wouldn’t have understood it at the level I do now. So in a way, it’s almost not worth saying.

But I would’ve tried to convey that as deeply as I could.

What are bad recommendations you hear about coaching or training volleyball?

Well, I think we’re going backwards a little bit in coaching. I think the content of coaching has exploded, and the quality has gone down—and I think those two things are directly correlated.

And I’m not trying to be like an old geezer slamming social media, but there’s a lot of effort right now going into how I look, how I sound, how I’m presenting. Which is fine—that’s a part of coaching.

But there’s a rigor to coaching that’s diminished, in my opinion, compared to 20 years ago when you didn’t have social media.

Back then, if you wanted to learn how to coach, you had to read a book, go to a clinic, and have an in-depth conversation. Someone would scrutinize you.

And I’m saying this from experience. I can think of five coaches off the top of my head from 20 years ago who I vehemently disagreed with, but who were phenomenal coaches. I might not have agreed with them, but they would push back, they’d challenge me, and they had real reasons for what they were doing.

I greatly respected the depth. Even if I disagreed with their conclusions, I respected the rigor it took to get there.

I’m not seeing that as much now, except from people who are already part of that older generation. Because again, so much of coaching right now is about how I sound when I’m talking, how I look when I’m talking, how I’m being presented.

And the stuff I’m seeing on social media? I mean, just empirically, scientifically, it’s wrong. But it sounds good, it looks good, and it’s produced well. So it gets eaten up.

So I don’t even know where to start with that question, is kind of what I’m saying. I’m just... yeah. Just saying what I’m seeing.

Do you have a particular goal? Maybe a goal that you already reached or a goal that is so hard to reach that it gives you daily motivation?

I'm not driven; I used to be, but I’m not driven by a particular goal anymore. I mean, of course, I have goals for the program. But what still gets me excited is what the job provides on a daily basis.

Like, I still get fired up about trying to figure out a better way to design a practice—and then seeing someone get better because of it.

It could be me and an assistant coach, or just anyone in the program, but when that spark happens in the day, when there’s a little leap forward, it gets me.

Whether it’s from the way practice was designed, or a meaningful conversation, or both coming together in the same session… those are the things that still fire me up.

I don’t know if I have specific goals, exactly, but I feel connected to that process. I’m shooting for that every day. So maybe it’s more of a target than a goal.

Of course, we do have goals. But I’d be lying if I said that’s what gets me out of bed. It’s the tiny sparks, those little moments that come from the work we’re doing, that still make this job feel like a cool thing.

Team culture. Do you have any methods that you return to every new season? It's a big question, but you can take your answer wherever you want to take it.

I'm not very good at long answers. I think, for us at least, there are team rules, there are team values, and then there’s the culture that’s created because of all that.

The rules, I mean, I think most coaches, way better than me, would agree with this, but I had a coach early in my career who said, “Hey, do you want to be a coach, or do you want to be a cop?” And I said, “I want to be a coach.” And he said, “Okay, then keep your rules small.” That always stuck with me.

It was Duncan McFarland; he was a great player and a great coach. And I think the rules have to be simple. The more rules you have, the more time you’ll spend enforcing them. And that’s not what we’re trying to do. We’re not cops.

So for me, I’ve got three or four core rules, max.

Then there are the values. And I can only speak for myself here, but for me, it’s about introducing those values and then, throughout the season, constantly pointing to them: “That’s an example of it.” “That’s not an example.” Over and over. And that’s just life. That’s how they get stronger.

And then, finally, that’s where culture comes in.

I think of it in layers: rules, then values, and then culture. And culture is just the result of all of it, the culmination of all the inputs, all the interactions, all the days stacked together across a season.

Honestly, I feel a little anxious the first time I give a talk on a value. Because if I don’t feel at least a little vulnerable when I talk about it, it’s probably not the right value.

But even then, the talk isn’t the most important part. What really matters are those hundred little interactions during practice. That’s where it becomes real, or it doesn’t.

That’s where the culture either forms or it doesn’t.

Matias: Do you expect your assistants to call out and reinforce these values whenever they notice them, or do you prefer to handle that yourself?

Is everyone on the staff free to point things out and clarify?
Or is that mainly your role?

No, I think it’s two parts to that.

First, for sure. The better I am at understanding our values and how they’re implemented, and the better I am at communicating that, the better my assistants will be, the better the players will be... and on and on. No question.

But the second part is this: I do think there’s a slippery slope.

We all have egos, we just do. And there’s a part of competitive sports that I don’t think you can be great at unless you have some kind of ego. There are a lot of ways that can show up, but it has to be there in some form.

And I know, speaking as someone who's been a younger coach, it’s incredibly hard to go in with a value that you don’t fully understand, and admit to yourself that you don’t fully understand it.

That’s hard. Nobody wants to look bad.

And I’ve gotten caught in traps where I thought I understood a value better than I did. And then I’d talk about it with way more certainty than I should’ve. And people pick up on that fast.

Suddenly, I’m enforcing something, not because it’s about the value, but because I’m enforcing it. It becomes about me. And that’s a slippery slope.

It’s so important to be able to step back and recognize, “Okay, this is about me, not the value.” The quicker we can catch that, the quicker we can build the kind of culture we want.

And maybe this is a third layer, but it’s something I’d tell myself: You have to make those mistakes. That’s part of being human. You’re going to screw up. You’re going to have moments where your ego gets louder than your values. And that’s okay. That’s part of the journey.

Honestly, I don’t know how much slack we allow ourselves for that sometimes. But that’s something I’d try to remind myself of: the mistakes are part of it. Just recognize them, talk about how you’re going to adjust, and move on.

I think I used to beat myself up way too much over those things.

When preparing a new game, what does your general approach look like, and have you ever changed your approach? Why did you change, and was the outcome beneficial?

I mean, there’s been an evolution.

In general, I think scouting reports are probably the biggest violation of the 80/20 rule of all time.

If you look at the amount of time we tend to spend on them during the week versus the actual return on investment we get... it doesn’t make sense. But we do it anyway.

Why? Well, one, because we care. We're coaches, we want to feel prepared, we want to feel busy. And you can bury yourself in a hundred hours of scouting if you want. Also, there’s a fear of not being prepared. And again, that fear comes from a good place; you care about your team, you want to do right by them.

But for me, and this is just my opinion, the most common time-allocation mistake coaches make is spending 20 to 40 hours a week charting opponent hitters and designing a detailed blocking plan. I don’t know if there’s a more negative ROI situation in coaching. The time spent just doesn’t match the actual impact on the game.

Now, I started there too. And honestly, I don’t even think it’s a bad thing for a young coach to go through that phase. There’s learning and evolution in that process. But what’s often missing is an honest look at cause and effect.

Are we allocating our time based on actual impact? Or are we doing it out of fear, or just habit?

There was one moment, a story I wish I could share in detail, where I was in a room with some incredibly smart coaches. We spent a huge amount of time building a scouting report. Then I saw how little impact it had on the match. That was the moment I made a hard turn in how I approach this stuff.

Over time, I’ve come to believe that the biggest value in scouting reports is on the offensive and serving side. How are we going to attack your block? How are we going to serve you? That’s where the real impact is.

Because let’s be honest: most points in a set come from kills. So, how we’re creating those opportunities, that’s where our focus should be.

I still think scouting reports matter. Absolutely. There are those moments, once or twice a year, where your plan fits perfectly and has an outsized impact on a match. That does happen. And that’s worth pursuing.

But we need to be clear-eyed about where the real gains are coming from.

And one last thing—something I think is overlooked: we spend an unbelievable amount of time on the scouting report itself, especially on blocking... and very little time on the debrief or “autopsy” afterward.

I would flip that completely.

The debrief, what happened, why it happened, where we got gains, where we didn’t, that’s incredibly valuable. I think all great organizations take time to reflect like that. But in most coaching staffs, it's flipped: tons of time on scouting, very little time on reflection. And that model just keeps you stuck in the same loop.

I think in coaching, there’s this constant balance between intuition and analytics, right? And we need both.

Intuition only comes through experience. You get a coach who’s been at a high level, and you've had so many of them on this podcast, and they can just see things the rest of us can’t. They make decisions based on that experience. We all know that.

But the hard part about doing the autopsy, the reason it doesn’t get done very often, is that even if you’ve been coaching for 45 years, there’s still a limit to what you can see without going back and combing through the data. The game just moves too fast. The volume of information is too big. You have to do the work to extract the lessons, and that takes time.

And here’s the thing: it’s not fun. It’s tedious. So what happens is, we start to overvalue our intuition, because we just don’t want to do the grind.

That’s why I think this should be encouraging for younger coaches. Maybe you don’t have elite intuition yet, but you can outwork people on this.

You can go deep on the film, the data, and the reflection. You can learn.

And the best coaches? They never stop doing that work. You have interviewed more than a couple who do and did an exceptional work there.

I remember sitting at this desk with Jim McLaughlin, who’s retired, doesn’t have to be doing any of it, and he was walking me through how he debriefs a match. He was showing me what he knew. I didn’t even have the reference points to know what was meaningful and what wasn’t.

He’d draw a line, then say, “No, no, no,” erase it, draw it again. To me, it looked the same, but to him, it was different. That kind of detail. That level of attention.

I loved watching that. It was such a good reminder of what being great looks like. It’s not just the intuition, it’s the discipline to keep doing the work. To never stop grinding.

To me, that’s the highest level.

The future of volleyball, from a technical point of view, where would you like to see the game evolve to ? What is needed to get there?

I think there’s like a few layers I wanted to hit: I’d like to take it to the world stage, then talk about what’s happening in America (there’s some overlap there), then I’d like to just mention two quick rules, and finally, the youth level and how that impacts everything.

So first, worldwide, volleyball is in a unique situation as a sport. And this overlaps with the U.S., too. Volleyball is massive in other countries. Massive in participation, money, and attention. There are multiple countries where the sport has high earnings, high participation, and high media coverage. That’s kind of the trifecta we’re aiming for.

Then you look at America. High participation numbers, for sure, but low media attention and, generously, low-to-middle earning potential.

So I think globally, if we want the sport to keep growing, we need to find a way to better align with what’s happening in the U.S. because some countries are thriving in all three areas.

And one simple, practical step would be to streamline the FIVB schedule with the NCAA season.

Right now, it’s not working. The NCAA says, “We don’t care what the rest of the world’s doing,” and the rest of the world says the same back. But the reality is, too many major tournaments happen during the NCAA season, and that affects the participation of top players and coaches. It limits U.S. involvement.

In the rest of the world, you can be a pro coach and still coach for your national team. That’s not possible here. There’s a big conflict.

I know how it sounds. It might sound like I’m saying, “Hey, everyone shift for us,” but I’m not saying that as a pigheaded American. I’m just saying that if we care about growing the sport globally, the American calendar matters. It has to be part of the conversation.

No, it’s not going to change in the next three months. But if the NCAA and FIVB would just talk, maybe some long-term alignment could start to happen.

And this ties into the second part, more on the American side, but we need everyone in women’s volleyball to start advocating way more for the sport. And to do it consciously.

We’re quiet right now. I think people who have influence are, in general, happy that they have it, and they’re staying in their lanes. Some players could be using their voices to talk about what’s happening in the sport, but I see them using their platforms more to highlight their journeys, which is great, but...

Things are happening right now in U.S. volleyball that aren’t good. And I think a lot of us can see it happening, but no one’s saying much. I fear that we’re going to start speaking up only once the damage is done.

We need to be more organized. We need to get out of our lanes and start advocating for the sport. And I’m not just putting this on women, this is on everyone involved in the women’s game.

I do think it’s going to happen, but probably not until the pain is already here. I just wish we were more practical, because we can all see it coming.

Okay, quick rule changes, two that drive me crazy.

One: the down ref should be on the other side. The up ref should be next to the coach. There’s no other sport I know of where the head coach and the top ref can’t directly communicate. In volleyball, we not only put the head ref away from the coach, but we also make it illegal to talk to them. I know volleyball has a very polite culture, but I’d change that immediately.

Second, the throws and carries in the men’s game have gone too far. It’s gotten to the point, especially a couple of years ago, where even a casual fan could watch and say, “That doesn’t look like volleyball. That looks like playing catch.” Now, watching the most recent Olympics, I do think the men are hitting more, and the impact isn’t quite as bad, but still. I don’t think that’s good for the game.

I think the more explosive the game is, the more fan-friendly it becomes.

Also, just too many breaks. Too many timeouts, and they’re too long. I’d change that too. The rule stuff I’m talking about, especially the second and third, is really about improving the fan experience.

Last piece, youth volleyball.

This is mainly in the States, since I don’t know as much about the overseas scene. But the game is getting so structured, so early. And it’s complicated. I don’t love it.

We’re putting kids into highly structured, non-game-like environments way too early. And I don’t think that’s good for development or enjoyment. It’s complicated how to fix it, but if I had fairy godmother powers and could wave a wand, I’d start there.

Best game you've ever seen. What recent or game from the volleyball vaults validated for you once more that volleyball is the greatest sport on the globe?

Well, I do think it is the greatest sport, the indoor game. I didn’t get to watch it live. I’ve just watched it on film, and honestly, I’ve watched it more than any game of anything I’ve ever seen.

But the 2008 gold medal match, where the USA beat Brazil in the men’s final, that one stands out.

For a lot of reasons. Ron Larsen was the assistant coach on that team, and he’s been a huge mentor of mine. And the whole story around that team is just incredible.

I mean, he had to step in and be the head coach for two games because Hugh McCutcheon had the tragedy with his wife’s family.

And Brazil had such a stranglehold on the men’s game for so long, so that match meant so much, on so many different levels.

And the execution in that game... I mean, I know how hard the USA team worked to play that style of volleyball. And Brazil was playing their style, the one they’d perfected.

It was just... everything about that game felt like it was more than just the people on the court.

I didn’t get to see it live; it was like one in the morning when it aired, but I can watch that match over and over and over. And I think that’s because I know so many of the people who were coaching and playing, and I know what it took.

It’s just really incredible.

It’s everything. The story behind that game. The tradition of those two teams. Where they both were in history at that time. And just the level of execution that everyone was playing at, it was just an incredible match for me to watch.

Gold medal match - Beijing 2008

The other one is a beach match, the 1996 Olympics in Atlanta. It was a pool play game when Karch and Kent beat Carl Henkel and Sinjin in three sets.

The level of animosity between those teams... the controversy around giving one of the seeds to Sinjin... and then Henkel blocks Karch like seven times, and Karch still finds a way to come back.

That match was insane.

That one I did watch live, because everyone was watching it. Everyone knew it was a grudge match. It was just one of the most exciting games. Even now, I still get goosebumps watching it on YouTube. It’s pretty incredible.

So I think those are the two matches that stand out for me.

Atlanta 1996 - Winner's bracket 4th round

Matias: “When we were talking before about dumping, and shotting and the rules getting too lean and maybe even giving advantages to smaller spikers again, I was thinking about exceptional smaller outside hitter who made a mark on the game while being undersized, and I thought about Riley Salmon, and you just mentioned that game, and those Olympics where he reached an incredible level.

Well, just a quick share, because I want to mention him, Ken Stanley, who was a longtime coach at Pierce College. He’s the one who really kind of taught me everything I know about coaching.

He coached Riley at Pierce College, and I think Riley’s one of the few players who went straight from playing at Pierce to the national team. He didn’t play at a four-year.

But I definitely want to mention Ken "Big Daddy" Stanley, his name, and he’s struggling right now. He might not pull through, so I really want to make sure he’s in here.

Tom's short-form tribute to Ken "Big Daddy" Stanley.

You’re right. I’ve coached at least 10 players who I’ve made watch that game, specifically Riley, maybe 20 times. I’ve had players who are sick of watching it because I’ve made them watch Riley so many times.

If you can make the strongest 'starting six' in the world. What would your team look like? Why did you choose these players?

I’m not great with current names, so I’m just gonna throw out a few, just for fun. Different people, different positions.

Setter — for sure, Luciano De Cecco. That guy’s incredible. I love watching him. He was so ahead of his time. Just a savant.

Outside hitter — one of them would have to be Karch Kiraly, for sure. And I think the other would be Giba. Both of them in their primes.

Opposite — that one’s tough. There are a few. I mean, I think of Pat Powers, Steve Timmons, Clayton Stanley… so many great international opposites. It’s hard to pick just one.

Middle — I always really respected David Lee and how he went about it. And Danielle Scott-Arruda. Dani’s the one. She’s just… a unicorn. Her skill level, competitive greatness, personal character, it’s like, how did this person even get born? She’s still such a role model.

Libero — that one’s tough. It’s hard to find someone who’s truly elite as both a receiver and a defender. The position’s so specialized, so it comes down to whether they’re great at those two things.

I don’t know if I have a single name that stands out, but obviously, I’m fond of Kayla Banwarth; I coached her, and she’s doing great now. And then Rich Lambourne he played in that match I talked about earlier. He was incredible in that game.

But yeah, there are a lot of great ones.

If you could have a gigantic billboard anywhere with anything on it — metaphorically speaking, getting a message out to every athlete on the globe — what would it say and why?

I don’t know if I have a big takeaway or closing thought. I guess my sense of urgency right now, is just to advocate more for our sport.

It’s not just about using your role in volleyball as leverage for yourself. We have to start advocating for the sport itself.

And I get it, I’m speaking as an American here, but even though women’s volleyball is really big right now, if you look at how a lot of other women’s sports have grown in the U.S., it’s usually because there’s a men’s equivalent they can point to. And we’re kind of the opposite.

So if we want volleyball to be at the level we all want it to be at in the States, I think that’s not just good for the U.S., I think it’s good for the whole world.

That’s why I think advocating more for the status of our sport, and where we want it to go, and being intentional about that, is so important. I believe we’re at a pivotal moment in the U.S., and what we do now has a ripple effect on everything.

How has a failure, or apparent failure, set you up for later success? Do you have a “favorite failure” of yours?

I mean, I’ve got so many. I kind of get, honestly, I get overwhelmed thinking about all my failures and mistakes, and trying to pick one. I’ve got a lot that are not my favorites, for sure.

But one that still stands out to me, we were at Loyola Marymount, the school I was at before here. I think it was year three or four of the program. We started at rock bottom and had been building it up, and we were finally in a position that year to make the tournament. That was a huge jump from where we started.

We’d just had a really big win earlier that week, and then we were playing another team, a solid team, but kind of one of those where if we beat them, it doesn’t help us that much, but if we lose, it hurts us.

So we go into that match, and of course, we lose the first two sets. Of course.

And I wrote the wrong lineup for the third set.

And I knew. I mean, I’m not going to admit it in the moment, but I kind of knew right then that all the external stuff, all the pressure, had distracted me enough to do that. And you see that sometimes, even at the Olympics, day one, there’s always an out-of-rotation call because the pressure's more than we think. It happens.

But it was a pretty intense moment for me. We were finally in a position to make the tournament, and this wasn’t just about that game; it was about the years it took to get there.

And losing that game could knock us right back out. We'd just had a big win, and now I’m letting it get to me, to the point where I mess up the lineup.

And that switch could seal it.

I was almost in tears, I was so upset. And then... we not only ended up winning the game, but when they interviewed the other coach afterward, he credited me for making a gutsy lineup change.

And here’s the thing: I do think that switch had a big impact on the game. It was a simple change, just two players, but I don’t think I would’ve made that switch except by mistake.

So it’s one of those moments with more questions than takeaways.

I definitely made a mistake in how I got to that decision. I let the moment get too big. I was rehearsing my apology speech to the team during the set.

It was messy. It was a happy accident, in some ways, but not a good accident in terms of process. You can’t let your decision-making get to that point. That’s something that’s always stayed with me, on multiple levels.

In the last five years, what new belief, behaviour, or habit has most improved your life?

I think I’m always shaving away at habits and trying to refine what I’m doing. That’s just constant for me.

But there was a pretty big shift for me during COVID. The impact COVID was having on our players, on our program… I was handling it the way I had always coached. And it was just failure upon failure upon failure, to the point where it was spreading into my whole life outside of coaching.

So there was a massive philosophical shift for me coming out of that time.

At one level, it was as simple as, like, “focus more on the relationships.” But for me, that felt a little too cliché to mean something.

So the real shift was more like: stop looking so far outside, most of the problems are inside.

And that doesn’t mean stop searching or stop studying. I’m always going to be drawn to learning from people, connecting with people, that’s never going to change.

But what I started to see is that almost everything that’s going wrong is going wrong because of what’s going on inside me.

So post-COVID, it’s been this never-ending deep dive, almost like an excavation, into my personal faults and failings, and how those are showing up in my coaching and in how I impact other people.

That’s been the biggest shift. And yeah, it’s led to a lot of little habits and behaviors changing, but it’s not really about the habits. It’s about where those things are coming from and why I’m doing them.

So yeah—that’s the biggest shift.

Do you consider yourself more of a writer or a coach, and how do creativity and writing play a role in your life?

I’m a writer, also. I just make a lot more money coaching than I do writing. But I love thinking about art and sport, and how they cross over, and what the parallels and differences are.

There’s not a direct correlation between the two, and there shouldn’t be, but I get energized thinking about that.

Creativity is... we all have it. I believe that. And I think, just like with sports, we don’t all have it in equal measure, but if I were going to leap, I’d say 90% of us are way more creative than we think we are.

And tapping into that, it’s only going to help. Not just your work, but your life. Just the straight enjoyment of life, you know?

So yeah, in terms of books and reading, there’s all that, but the writing itself has been a really meaningful process for me in a lot of ways.

When you feel overwhelmed or unfocused, or have lost your focus temporarily, what do you do?

This is part of trying to learn about myself better, just the way I work.

I mean, of course, I get overwhelmed like anybody. But for me, it’s not really about a lack of focus. I can focus for a long period of time. If anything, my issue is the opposite; I double down on the focus to the point where I burn myself out.

And when that happens, a lot of my worst traits start leaking out in other areas.

So it's more about trying to be self-aware enough to recognize that as a trap for me, and to pull myself out of it. I have to make sure I’ve got activities in place that help me fill the tank back up.

Because I can focus so hard that I start isolating people. I get irritable. The main thing almost becomes too much of the main thing. I don’t leave any space around it.

So yeah, my problem’s a little bit the other way around. And that’s led to some problems, for sure.
