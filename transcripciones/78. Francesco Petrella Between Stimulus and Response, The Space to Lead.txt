78. Francesco Petrella: Between Stimulus and Response, The Space to Lead

Francesco Petrella's passion for volleyball, along with an expertise in team culture and leadership, and his experiences in the Italian Superlega, make him the perfect coach to have an interview with.

Quickly advancing through youth coaching roles, coach Petrella became the assistant coach of Modena's Superlega team in 2016.

In 2017 coach Petrella joined sides with Italian master coach Angelo Lorenzetti at Trentino Volley.

This was the start of a 6 year-long mentor-mentee relationship with one of the most respected coaches in the game. Together they won the Italian Championship, the World Club Championships, the CEV Cup, and more...

In the 2023-2024 season, Francesco became the head coach of his hometown team of Modena.

In this Masterclass coach Petrella talks about:

What living the pro coach life really means

The crucial elements that go into becoming a winning team

How standards, not rules, are necessary for team cohesion

How standards are monitored and upheld

Which roles and positions are needed in today's game

The focus on truth and clarity of reality inside a team

Rule changes that would benefit our sport

and so much more

Francesco, which advice would you give yourself as a starting coach? An advice that is so clear and logic for you now, but that you had to develop over the years.

One thing I always try to remember, especially in such a competitive environment, is the reason behind choosing to be a coach. It's crucial to remember that it’s more about pursuing your passion than focusing on the immediate outcomes of your career.

Another important aspect is not to rush decisions. Coaching is a long process, and each season, you must reaffirm your commitment to this profession since it’s not guaranteed.

Unlike other professions, where you might remain in the same role for decades, professional coaching requires more adaptation. You might coach professionally for a few years, then find yourself without a team, and then start over with a new team.

It's essential to view your coaching career as a marathon, not a sprint.

In a marathon, your pace at any given moment is important, but not as crucial as your overall endurance and strategy.

Matias: The differences between coaching professional teams in Europe and the collegiate system in the U.S., it's clear that the European system poses unique challenges. The huge emphasis on results-driven coaching, which is different from other environments where you might have more stability and control.

The only real decision you have control over at the end of each season is whether you want to continue being a professional coach the following year.

I recall reading a chapter in Carlo Ancelotti’s book, "Leader Calmo," where he discusses his experiences getting fired and how he navigated his career trajectory differently as he matured.

Ancelotti mentions that earlier in his career, he took firings personally, but with more experience and several trophies later, he learned to detach and not to dwell too deeply on the ups and downs, recognizing them as part of the game.

This approach helps conserve energy—energy better spent on daily coaching duties rather than fretting over every high and low.

What are bad recommendations you hear about coaching or training volleyball?

For me, it's not about one specific aspect of the game. I believe you can achieve success in many different ways, which is why the game is always evolving. For example, in professional volleyball, the importance of serving has increased significantly over the last decade.

This change has forced adjustments in reception tactics to counteract the evolution of service strategies.

This is why I don't believe there's only one way to play volleyball or one way to win games. Certain aspects are undoubtedly crucial, but there is no single, definitive method.

As coaches, sometimes we assert that our approach is the only way, which isn't correct. We tend to do this because players often need to feel that sense of decisive leadership, and it’s a way for them to feel secure behind the coach’s decisions.

There’s an Italian saying, "pensiamo alla prossima," which means "just think about the next ball." This concept can be frustrating because it suggests always moving on without considering the importance of the present moment. The reality is that not every situation can be overlooked; some moments require immediate and thoughtful decisions based on the current context, not just what’s next.

In coaching, instilling the importance of focusing intensely on the present can increase pressure, not only on the players but also on coaches and clubs. However, I am convinced that teams which maintain this level of intensity consistently are the ones that achieve success.

So, you have to decide what you aim for. If you aspire to be a winning team, you must embrace and sustain this intensity.

If comfort is your preference, that's also a choice, but it won't align with achieving significant success.

What is the most important key factor in creating culture to you?

I believe that the most essential word in team sports, particularly in volleyball, is "responsibility." In Italian, "responsabilità" - abilita a rispondere - means the ability to respond effectively to a situation.

This is crucial because it places the emphasis on the need to take charge at pivotal moments. In individual sports, it's simpler; you're by yourself and can only blame external factors, such as the referee in tennis.

But in team sports, assuming responsibility is optional, you can hide yourself in a team setting. Yet, in my view, a true champion always chooses to shoulder this responsibility.

When true champions face defeat, their reaction isn't to dwell on the loss; instead, they're prepared to immediately try again, much like inserting another coin into a slot machine after a loss.

This resilience sets apart the real champions from merely good or average players who might shy away from taking responsibility due to the emotional weight it entails.

If players continuously move from one moment to the next (which is what you instill if you say 'next ball') without processing their emotions—whether it's anger from a personal mistake or frustration due to a teammate's error—these emotions accumulate.

Over time, this can lead to an emotional outburst at a crucial moment in the game. By acknowledging and managing these emotions as they happen, players can be better prepared for significant moments in the season or in their lives.

Do you have a particular goal? Maybe a goal that you already reached or a goal that is so hard to reach that it gives you daily motivation?

As I mentioned before, to be a professional coach, you must have the daily desire to embrace that role.

It's my personal goal to commit to being a professional coach every day.

However, it's not entirely within my control—it also depends on my team, my players, and my staff. This aspect is crucial because I genuinely enjoy being in the gym with the people around me, whether they are players, staff, or other professionals.

I've learned to cherish this from my mentor, Angelo Lorenzetti. I used to spend long hours in the gym, from eight in the morning to eight at night, even on rest days, simply because I enjoy the company and the atmosphere.

To me, coaching is partly about the specifics of the sport, but it's equally about the relationships you develop with your players and the environment you're part of.

The experience of coaching goes beyond just playing the game; it’s about the transformative journey you undertake with your team. There's a profound satisfaction in leading an underdog team to victory against a stronger opponent.

The actual win is exhilarating, but there’s a moment during the match when you realize you are truly competing and have a chance to win—that's even more gratifying.

I believe the Superlega is one of the most prestigious leagues in our sport. That's why I wanna coach here.

Team management. Managing different teams year over year. Do you have any methods that you reach back to?

As a professional coach, when you join a new club in Italy, the first thing you should do is understand the organization, rather than imposing what you think is the best structure.

It’s crucial to recognize that you were chosen by the club, and not the other way around. You need to learn what resources and support the organization can offer you and then build your strategy from there, because every team is different.

To me, the most important thing is that the coach and the technical staff focus solely on their specific roles, like managing practices in the gym, and not get involved in the broader organizational aspects of the club. That's on an organizational level.

On the team level I prefer to meet with each member of the team individually before addressing the entire team together. This approach helps me understand each person’s desires and the key dynamics within the team. Not all 14 players in you team can be or want to be leaders.

It’s important to identify who the natural leaders are and who could potentially step up as leaders.

Each club has its unique communication styles and levels of ambition, so it's essential to first understand the club's culture and environment before you can effectively communicate with your team.

Regarding rules, I'm not fond of setting strict rules, especially with professional teams. You’re dealing with adults who understand basic expectations, like being punctual for practice. Typically, players handle the consequences of lateness among themselves in the locker room, so it’s unnecessary to enforce this as a formal rule.

Instead of rules, I prefer to establish standards.

There’s a great book by Coach K called "The Gold Standard" where he describes setting standards with the 2008 and 2012 U.S. Olympic basketball team. In it, he talks about an initial team meeting where everyone contributes to a set of standards they all agree to live by.

I believe setting a few clear, meaningful standards—and explaining why they are crucial—can make the difference between a successful season and a disappointing one. Every day, these standards should guide the team’s practices.

If you have the time to do this exercise, I believe it's the most powerful thing you can do.

I believe in setting standards rather than rules, as rules often end up being about assigning blame or imposing penalties, which isn’t as beneficial for team cohesion.

An obvious question for me to ask now is how you go about managing these standards and most importantly, which feedback do you provide to maintain the shared set of standards?

Firstly, I believe it's crucial to establish only a small number of standards. Having too many can be overwhelming and difficult to remember for everyone involved.

If you set a long list of standards, it becomes challenging to monitor and measure each one consistently. The standards you choose should be fundamental enough that they become the team's reasons for existing, necessitating their assessment on a daily basis.

A coach's responsibility is to diligently monitor these standards each day and maintain open communication with both the coaching staff and the players.

This communication should occur in two key ways: first, to highlight and reinforce when standards are being met successfully, helping the team understand what meeting these standards looks like. Secondly, it is just as important to communicate when standards are not being met.

This dual approach ensures that players understand they can rely on your leadership to uphold these standards consistently.

By managing this balance, you help build trust and accountability within the team, reinforcing the importance of each standard set.

According to you, what rule change is necessary? And why would you want to see this rule implemented?

For starters, double touches a lot of people find confusing, and honestly, I don’t see the point in keeping it.

And then there’s the substitution rule—it could be more flexible. It might be interesting to shake things up a bit, see if a change in substitutions could bring a new dynamic to the game.

There's also been talk about changing the game format to seven sets of 21 points each. I’m not sure if it'll make the sport more engaging or not, but changing the rules every four years, like after an Olympic cycle, could be quite fascinating.

I live in Modena, the hometown of Ferrari, I really admire how Formula One updates its rules every few years to challenge the teams. It keeps things fresh, seeing how they adapt and innovate in response to new rules.

Another thing I think could really change our game for the better is reducing the downtime between plays. If you compare volleyball to basketball, or specifically the NBA, the overall game time isn’t that different.

But basketball has a flow that keeps the action going despite more frequent breaks. In volleyball, we hit the ball three times and then everyone just stands around for 25 seconds before getting back to it. This stop-and-go really breaks the rhythm.

Cutting down on these breaks could make our matches a lot more dynamic and exciting.

You need to create a high performing team for next season. How would you want that team to look like?

To me, the first thing you need to build a strong team is having powerful servers. Ideally, you want four good servers. Having at least four ensures that in every set, you're likely to have at least two servers who can really put pressure on the opponent and score breakpoint opportunities.

Next up is passing ability, which is crucial. If you've got skilled passers, your chances of scoring on the side-out increase significantly.

Being good at passing doesn’t necessarily mean perfect receptions every time; it’s more about not losing points on the pass and maintaining high efficiency.

Then, there’s the relationship between the setter and the middle blockers. Having at least one very strong middle attacker is essential, especially if your reception isn’t always on point. With the size of blockers these days, a capable middle attacker can make a huge difference.

They make life easier for your hitters by creating more opportunities to score.

It's like trying to open a safe—you start with the middle blocker. If you’ve got that position solidified, it’s like you’ve got the first number to the combination, which makes everything else fall into place.

If you could have a gigantic billboard anywhere with anything on it — metaphorically speaking, getting a message out to every coach on the globe — what would it say and why?

I really appreciate a quote from Pep Guardiola, the famous soccer coach. He said "coaching is so exciting because your brain needs to go at full speed all the time".

I understand that being a youth coach and a professional coach aren't the same. The pressures are different, the stakes are different, but if you genuinely want to enjoy being a professional coach, you have to love this aspect of always being on.

You're constantly thinking of different strategies for practice, different approaches for your players, solutions for matches, about the mistakes you've made.

You really have to enjoy living it this way.

What has been your favorite failure?

Losing two consecutive Championship finals was tough, but the first loss was particularly challenging.

The month following that defeat was incredibly difficult. There was a major shakeup at the club, and many players left for other teams. It felt like Angelo and I were left alone. However, this situation actually brought us closer and strengthened our resolve.

Last year, we won the Italian championship, and looking back, I believe us sticking together during that challenging period played a big part in our victory. I'm not sure if we learned something new that we hadn't known before, but I do know that it gave both of us a stronger reason to achieve something great.

That challenging time was crucial for us and was a key factor in our success two years later.

Which matchups have you enjoyed the most? From your own experience as a coach, or as a spectator?

My favorite game, speaking from my experience, was definitely the last game of the previous season, Gara Cinque Finale Scudetto. That game was incredibly special to me because it was the decisive match where Angelo and I, staying with the same team, won the Italian championship.

The emotional intensity of that moment, knowing it was the last game and winning it all, made it incredibly significant for me.

Similarly, another deeply memorable game for me was when we won the championship in Modena seven years ago. Modena is my hometown, so winning the championship there was simply phenomenal. These are undoubtedly the highlights of my career.

On the topic of volleyball quality, one recent match that stands out to me was the Milano versus Perugia semifinal in the Scudetto (Italian Championship). It was an incredibly intense match with a very high level of play.

Last year, we also had a similarly intense match in Trento, which might have even surpassed it in terms of scoring drama. The Champions League quarterfinal, a six-set thriller against the Polish Champions of Zaksa.

We played five regular sets followed by a golden set, that entire game was an extremely intense experience.

Do you have a book that you like a lot? Which lessons did you get out of it?

For me, the most impactful in terms of personal growth was Mindset by Carol Dweck.

It acted like a key, unlocking insights from every other book I read after that.

I believe it's a must-read for anyone looking to advance in any leadership-oriented role in their life.

Over the years, I've read a lot of books, though I've had to cut back recently due to having less free time.

I like to reflect on what might be missing in my life or my work and then seek out books that address those gaps or are related to topics I want to explore further.

In the last five years, what habit has most improved your life?

As I've mentioned before, one critical aspect of coaching is managing relationships with players.

Ideally, every coach wants to inspire their players, but realistically, that's not always possible.

With a team of 14 or 15 players, it's challenging to be the same source of inspiration for everyone. You have to accept that some players might have different interests and perspectives from your own.

From my experience, one of the most significant changes—or improvements—I've made was learning to always confront the truth of any situation with my players.

This means not being afraid of conflicts, especially when they concern crucial team issues.

Often, dysfunction within a team stems from different interpretations of reality.

Whether it's between the coach and players, among the players themselves, or within the coaching staff, these differing viewpoints can create conflicts that are impossible to resolve unless everyone starts to see and accept the same reality.

So, it's vital to talk openly about situations and not hide anything.

Sometimes, we might hide things not out of fear or concern about how players might view us, but in the hope that these issues will somehow resolve themselves over time. Other times, we might withhold information to avoid hurting someone.

I firmly believe that discussing the reality of situations openly and clearly—even if it leads to conflicts—is essential.

This approach not only helps address issues more effectively but also reveals which players are willing to confront reality and which ones might be avoiding it.

When you feel overwhelmed or unfocused, or have lost your focus temporarily, what do you do?

I find that I do my best thinking when I'm alone, maybe in my office or even just sitting quietly in a park.

It’s my way to really reflect on what’s just happened. After that, I like to talk to someone I really trust—to listen to their take on the situation.

I usually take a few days to mull everything over before deciding on how to respond. I think it's important to be upfront about things that happen. It’s not about trying to erase a situation or hide from it. You’ve got to acknowledge it and give yourself some space to process.

This is similar to how I manage my team. I tell my players not to rush onto the next thing without taking a moment.

You need that space between when it happened and when you react to it. If you skip that part, you might react impulsively, and that reaction might not be the best one.

Taking your time helps ensure that your response is thoughtful and well-considered.

The ability to take that time between the stimulus and the reaction to me is very important, it's good leadership and it's also needed to find the best solution for the issue at hand.
